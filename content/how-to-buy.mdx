# How to Buy a Business in India: Step-by-Step Guide

*Last updated: February 2026*

**Buying a business in India follows a structured process: define your criteria, search for opportunities, evaluate targets, conduct due diligence, negotiate terms, complete legal documentation, and transition into ownership.** This guide walks you through each step with specific actions, timelines, and India-specific considerations.

---

## What Are the Steps to Buy a Business in India?

**The business acquisition process in India consists of seven key phases: criteria definition, search and screening, initial evaluation, due diligence, negotiation, closing, and transition.** The entire process typically takes 4-9 months, with each phase requiring specific activities and deliverables.

Understanding the full process before you begin helps set realistic expectations and prevents costly mistakes. Many first-time buyers underestimate the time required, rush through due diligence, or sign unfavorable agreements due to deal pressure. By following a structured approach, you maintain leverage throughout the process and make better decisions. The phases below form a linear progression, though in practice you may be evaluating multiple opportunities simultaneously during the search phase.

| Phase | Key Activities | Typical Duration |
|-------|---------------|------------------|
| 1. Define Criteria | Industry, size, location, budget | 1-2 weeks |
| 2. Search & Screen | Find opportunities, initial calls | 2-4 months |
| 3. Initial Evaluation | Review financials, site visits | 2-4 weeks per target |
| 4. Due Diligence | Deep investigation, verify claims | 4-8 weeks |
| 5. Negotiation | Price, terms, structure | 2-4 weeks |
| 6. Closing | Documentation, fund transfer | 2-4 weeks |
| 7. Transition | Seller handover, relationship building | 30-90 days |

## Step 1: How Do You Define Your Acquisition Criteria?

**Start by defining your ideal acquisition target based on four factors: industry, size (revenue and profit), location, and budget (total capital you can deploy).** Clear criteria prevent wasted time on unsuitable opportunities and help brokers and networks find relevant deals.

Your acquisition criteria should be specific enough to filter opportunities but flexible enough to not miss good deals. Consider your existing skills and experience—buying in an industry you understand reduces risk significantly. Think about your lifestyle preferences: do you want a business requiring daily presence, or one that can run with a manager? Be realistic about budget: your total capital should be roughly 40-50% of target business price to account for down payment, working capital, and transaction costs.

**Key Questions to Define Your Criteria:**

1. **Industry:** What sectors match your experience? What industries interest you?
2. **Size:** What revenue and profit range is realistic for your capital and skill?
3. **Location:** How far are you willing to travel? Remote management possible?
4. **Owner involvement:** Full-time operations or semi-absentee?
5. **Growth vs stability:** Turnaround project or steady cash flow?
6. **Budget:** How much can you invest without financial stress?

| Buyer Profile | Suggested Business Size | Total Capital Needed |
|---------------|------------------------|---------------------|
| First-time, employed | ₹15-40 lakh price | ₹10-20 lakh |
| Full-time entrepreneur | ₹40 lakh - ₹1.5 crore | ₹25-60 lakh |
| Experienced acquirer | ₹1-5 crore | ₹50 lakh - ₹2 crore |
| Investment group | ₹5-25 crore | ₹2-10 crore |

## Step 2: Where Do You Search for Businesses for Sale?

**Search for acquisition targets through online marketplaces (SMERGERS, IndiaMart, BizBuySell), business brokers, CA networks, industry associations, and direct outreach to business owners.** A multi-channel approach is essential since India lacks a centralized business-for-sale platform.

Expect the search phase to take 2-4 months and be the most time-consuming part of the process. You'll likely review 30-50 opportunities for every one that reaches the due diligence stage. Create a systematic approach: set up alerts on online platforms, schedule weekly calls with 2-3 brokers, and dedicate time for direct outreach. Don't limit yourself to actively listed businesses—many owners would consider selling but haven't listed. A respectful letter or LinkedIn message expressing acquisition interest can uncover hidden opportunities.

**Search Channels (Priority Order):**

1. **SMERGERS** — India's largest SME marketplace, good filtering options
2. **Business Brokers** — CAs and M&A advisors often have unlisted deals
3. **IndiaMart Business Transfer** — Strong for manufacturing, trading
4. **Industry Networks** — Trade associations, supplier referrals
5. **LinkedIn Outreach** — Direct approach to business owners
6. **CA/CS Referrals** — Professionals advising business exits
7. **BizBuySell/BusinessesForSale** — International platforms, franchise focus

**For each opportunity, track:**
- Business description and industry
- Asking price and stated profit
- Reason for sale
- Initial impression
- Next steps and timeline

## Step 3: How Do You Evaluate a Business Opportunity Initially?

**Initial evaluation involves reviewing seller-provided financials, understanding the business model, assessing owner dependency, and determining if the opportunity merits deeper investigation.** This phase filters opportunities before you invest significant time and money in due diligence.

Request a Confidential Information Memorandum (CIM) or similar overview document from the seller or broker. This should include business history, financial summary (3 years minimum), products/services, customer overview (without identifying details), and reason for sale. Analyze the numbers critically: calculate profit margins, revenue trends, and implied valuation multiple. Compare to industry benchmarks. Schedule a call with the seller to understand their motivation, transition willingness, and any information not in the documents. Visit the business location if possible—physical observation reveals much that documents cannot.

**Initial Evaluation Checklist:**

1. **Financial Review:**
   - Revenue trend (growing, stable, declining?)
   - Profit margins (realistic for industry?)
   - Asking price multiple (2-4x is normal for SMBs)
   - Working capital requirements

2. **Business Model Assessment:**
   - Customer concentration (any client >20% of revenue?)
   - Recurring vs one-time revenue
   - Competitive advantages
   - Growth potential

3. **Owner Dependency:**
   - How involved is the owner daily?
   - Key relationships held by owner vs company
   - Could the business run without owner for 2 weeks?

4. **Seller Motivation:**
   - Why are they selling?
   - What's their timeline?
   - Will they provide seller financing?
   - Willing to stay during transition?

| Red Flag | Significance | Action |
|----------|--------------|--------|
| Declining revenue (3+ years) | Structural problems | Investigate cause or pass |
| >40% revenue from one customer | High concentration risk | Factor into price/terms |
| Owner works 60+ hours/week | High owner dependency | Assess operability without owner |
| Vague financials | Possible hidden issues | Request detailed verification |
| Seller unwilling to finance | May doubt sustainability | Proceed with caution |
| Multiple failed sale attempts | Underlying problems | Understand why previous deals failed |

## Step 4: How Do You Make an Offer (Letter of Intent)?

**Once initial evaluation is positive, submit a Letter of Intent (LOI) outlining proposed purchase price, deal structure, key terms, and conditions including exclusivity and due diligence period.** The LOI is typically non-binding except for exclusivity and confidentiality provisions.

The LOI serves multiple purposes: it demonstrates serious buyer intent, establishes a framework for negotiations, and secures exclusivity while you conduct due diligence. Draft the LOI carefully—while most terms are non-binding, they set expectations for final negotiations. Include a valuation rationale to justify your offer price. Request exclusivity of 60-90 days to complete due diligence without competing buyers. Specify conditions that must be satisfied: satisfactory due diligence, financing approval, and any other key requirements.

**Standard LOI Components:**

1. **Purchase Price:** Proposed amount and how you arrived at it
2. **Deal Structure:** Asset purchase or share purchase
3. **Payment Terms:** Down payment, seller financing, earnout if any
4. **Exclusivity Period:** 60-90 days typically
5. **Due Diligence Scope:** What you'll investigate
6. **Conditions:** Financing, due diligence results, lease transfer, etc.
7. **Proposed Timeline:** Target closing date
8. **Confidentiality:** Binding provision on information received
9. **Non-Binding Nature:** Clarify which provisions are binding (usually just exclusivity and confidentiality)

**Sample Payment Structure (₹1 Crore Deal):**
- Signing deposit: ₹5 lakh (held in escrow)
- Down payment at closing: ₹25 lakh
- Seller financing: ₹40 lakh (4 years, 12% interest)
- Bank loan: ₹30 lakh (secured by assets)

## Step 5: What Is Involved in Due Diligence?

**Due diligence is the comprehensive investigation of all aspects of the business—financial, legal, operational, customer, and employee—conducted after LOI signing and before final commitment.** This phase typically takes 4-8 weeks and should be conducted with professional support.

Due diligence is your last opportunity to verify seller claims and discover hidden problems. Never skip or rush this phase regardless of deal pressure. Engage professionals early: a CA for financial due diligence, a lawyer for legal review, and potentially industry consultants for operational assessment. Create a due diligence request list and track document receipt systematically. Verify reported revenues against bank statements, GST returns, and tax filings. In Indian SMBs, discrepancies between books and actual cash flows are common—understand the full picture before proceeding.

**Financial Due Diligence:**
1. Income tax returns (3-5 years)
2. GST returns and compliance history
3. Bank statements (all accounts, 3 years)
4. Accounts receivable aging report
5. Accounts payable and debt schedule
6. Profit & loss statements (monthly if available)
7. Balance sheets with asset verification
8. Inventory valuation (physical count)
9. Working capital analysis
10. Cash flow statements

**Legal Due Diligence:**
1. Company formation documents
2. Shareholding pattern and changes
3. Board and shareholder resolutions
4. All licenses, permits, registrations
5. Property documents (owned or leased)
6. Pending litigation or disputes
7. Employee records and compliance (PF, ESI)
8. Major contracts and agreements
9. Intellectual property documentation
10. Insurance policies

**Operational Due Diligence:**
1. Customer interviews (top 5-10 customers)
2. Supplier relationships and contracts
3. Employee capabilities and retention risk
4. Equipment condition and maintenance records
5. Technology systems and data
6. Standard operating procedures
7. Quality metrics and customer complaints

## Step 6: How Do You Negotiate the Final Deal?

**Use due diligence findings to negotiate final purchase price, deal structure, representations and warranties, indemnification provisions, and transition arrangements.** Effective negotiation balances achieving favorable terms with maintaining a collaborative relationship for successful transition.

Due diligence often reveals issues that warrant price adjustments or structural changes. Approach negotiations as problem-solving rather than adversarial bargaining. Present findings factually and explain their impact on value or risk. Be willing to walk away if issues are significant and the seller won't adjust—sunk costs in due diligence are small compared to buying a problematic business. Consider creative structures that address both parties' concerns: earnouts for disputed value claims, escrow accounts for potential liabilities, or consulting agreements that align seller with your success.

**Common Negotiation Points:**

| Issue | Buyer Approach | Seller Approach | Typical Resolution |
|-------|---------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Price disagreement | Justify with comparable data, DD findings | Emphasize future potential | Earnout or seller financing |
| Hidden liabilities discovered | Price reduction or indemnification | Minimize significance | Escrow holdback |
| Customer concentration risk | Lower multiple or earnout | Long contracts, intros | Transition assistance agreement |
| Working capital shortfall | Additional capital adjustment | Include in price | Define target WC at closing |
| Key employee retention | Require contracts before closing | Can't guarantee | Retention bonuses funded by deal |

**Negotiation Leverage Points:**
- Thorough preparation (you understand the business better than most buyers)
- Willingness to walk away (never be desperate)
- Multiple options (continue evaluating other businesses)
- Seller's timeline pressure (retirement date, health issues)
- Financing alternatives (don't be dependent on seller financing)

## Step 7: What Documents Are Required to Close?

**The closing requires execution of the purchase agreement (Asset Purchase Agreement or Share Purchase Agreement), transfer documents for assets and licenses, and funds transfer through proper banking channels.** Engage an M&A lawyer to draft or review all documents.

The purchase agreement is the most important document—it defines exactly what you're buying, at what price, and with what protections. For asset purchases, you'll also need bills of sale, assignment agreements for contracts, and new applications for licenses that don't transfer. For share purchases, you'll need board resolutions, share transfer forms, and updated filings with the Registrar of Companies. Plan for closing day logistics: coordinate timing of document signing, funds transfer, and handover of physical assets and keys.

**Documents for Asset Purchase:**
1. Asset Purchase Agreement (APA)
2. Bill of Sale for movable assets
3. Assignment and Assumption Agreement (contracts)
4. Lease assignment or new lease
5. Non-compete agreement
6. Transition services agreement
7. Employment agreements (key staff)
8. IP assignment documents
9. Inventory transfer documents

**Documents for Share Purchase:**
1. Share Purchase Agreement (SPA)
2. Board resolution approving transfer
3. Share transfer forms (SH-4)
4. Updated MOA/AOA if needed
5. Shareholder agreement (if multiple)
6. Management/employment agreements
7. Non-compete agreement
8. Resignation letters (outgoing directors)

## Step 8: What Should You Do During the Transition Period?

**The post-closing transition period (typically 30-90 days) involves the seller introducing you to customers, suppliers, and employees while you learn operations and take over day-to-day management.** Plan and document the transition before closing—don't leave it to chance.

Structure the transition in your purchase agreement. Specify the seller's obligations: duration of availability, hours per week, activities included, and compensation if any. Create a transition checklist covering customer introductions, vendor relationships, operational training, system access handover, and key knowledge transfer. Document everything—the seller's institutional knowledge will fade quickly once they're gone. Plan for sellers to remain more involved initially (perhaps daily for week 1-2) then gradually reduce to as-needed consultation.

**Transition Timeline:**

| Timeframe | Focus Areas | Seller Involvement |
|-----------|-------------|-------------------|
| Week 1-2 | Introductions, daily operations | Full-time, side-by-side |
| Week 3-4 | Key processes, customer meetings | Half-time, available for questions |
| Month 2 | Independent operations, decision-making | Few hours/week consultation |
| Month 3 | Full autonomy, complex issues only | On-call as needed |

**Critical Transition Activities:**
1. Meet all employees individually
2. Introduction calls to top 20 customers
3. Meet key suppliers and vendors
4. Review all contracts and agreements
5. Learn financial systems and reporting
6. Document standard operating procedures
7. Take over banking and payment systems
8. Update signatory and access authorities

## How Much Time Does Each Step Take?

**The entire acquisition process from starting your search to completing transition takes 6-12 months, with the search phase being most variable depending on your criteria and market conditions.** Plan conservatively and don't let arbitrary deadlines push you into poor decisions.

## What Are Common Mistakes When Buying a Business?

**The most common mistakes first-time buyers make are: insufficient due diligence, overpaying based on seller projections rather than verified history, and underestimating working capital needs.** These mistakes are avoidable with proper preparation and professional support.

1. **Falling in love with a business:** Emotional attachment clouds judgment and weakens negotiating position
2. **Skipping due diligence:** Saves weeks but can cost crores in undiscovered problems
3. **Trusting seller financials:** Always verify with bank statements, tax returns, GST filings
4. **Overpaying:** Paying for potential rather than proven performance
5. **Ignoring cultural fit:** Different management styles can alienate employees and customers
6. **Underestimating working capital:** Businesses consume cash during ownership transitions
7. **Poor transition planning:** Knowledge loss when seller exits abruptly
8. **Skimping on professionals:** Lawyers and CAs cost lakhs, save crores

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Do I need a broker to buy a business in India?

**No, you don't need a broker, but working with one can significantly accelerate your search and provide access to off-market opportunities.** Brokers earn their fee (typically 2-5% of transaction value) by aggregating opportunities, handling initial screening, and facilitating negotiations. For buyers with limited time or industry networks, brokers provide valuable services. However, you can also search independently through online platforms, direct outreach, and professional networks. Many buyers use a hybrid approach: work with brokers for certain deal sizes or industries while conducting independent searches simultaneously. If you use a broker, clarify their fee structure, exclusivity requirements, and whether they represent the seller (most do).

### How do I know if a business is priced fairly?

**A business is priced fairly when the asking price reflects 2-4x verified annual profits (SDE or EBITDA) adjusted for risk factors like customer concentration, owner dependency, and industry trends.** Calculate the implied multiple: divide asking price by annual profit. Compare this multiple to industry benchmarks and similar recent transactions. A ₹50 lakh asking price for a business with ₹15 lakh verified annual profit implies 3.3x multiple—reasonable for a stable business with diversified customers. The same price for ₹10 lakh profit implies 5x—high unless there's strong growth trajectory or strategic value. Due diligence will reveal adjustments to stated profits that affect your fair value calculation.

### Should I buy through an asset purchase or share purchase?

**For most small business acquisitions under ₹2 crore, asset purchase is recommended because it provides better protection against inheriting unknown liabilities.** In an asset purchase, you buy specific assets (equipment, inventory, customer lists, brand) and leave the legal entity with the seller. Any undisclosed liabilities remain the seller's problem. The downside: you must transfer each license and contract individually, and some licenses may not be transferable. Share purchase is simpler for continuity—you buy the company itself with all assets, contracts, and licenses intact—but you inherit all liabilities, known or unknown. Larger deals, especially those involving many contracts or non-transferable licenses, often use share purchase with extensive representations, warranties, and indemnification for liability protection.

### What financing options are available for buying a business in India?

**Business acquisitions in India are typically financed through buyer equity (25-50%), seller financing (20-40%), and bank loans (15-40% for asset-backed portions).** Unlike developed markets, India lacks dedicated SBA-style acquisition loans, making seller financing often essential. Banks will lend against specific collateral—equipment, inventory, property—but rarely against "goodwill" or business value. Loan Against Property (LAP) is commonly used, where buyers pledge personal or family property as security. Some NBFCs offer business acquisition loans with higher rates (14-18%). For deals above ₹2 crore, institutional investors or family offices may participate, taking equity stakes in exchange for capital and often strategic value.

### Can I buy a business while still employed?

**Yes, many buyers acquire businesses while employed, starting with a semi-absentee model before transitioning to full-time ownership.** Look for businesses that can operate with a manager in place or require limited owner involvement (20-30 hours/week). Service businesses, franchise operations, and e-commerce businesses often fit this profile. Be transparent with yourself about time availability—underestimating required involvement is a common mistake. Plan for a transition period where you may need to take leave or work reduced hours. Some buyers negotiate a longer transition period with the seller specifically to accommodate their employment situation during handover.
