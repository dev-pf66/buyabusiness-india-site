# Due Diligence Checklist for Buying a Business in India

*Last updated: February 2026*

**Due diligence is the comprehensive investigation of a business before finalizing an acquisition—examining financial records, legal compliance, operations, customers, and employees to verify seller claims and uncover hidden risks.** This checklist covers everything you need to verify when buying a business in India.

---

## What Is Due Diligence When Buying a Business?

**Due diligence is the buyer's investigation period between signing a Letter of Intent (LOI) and closing the purchase, typically lasting 4-8 weeks, where you verify every material claim the seller has made about the business.** This is your last opportunity to discover problems before committing your capital.

Think of due diligence as an audit of everything that matters. You're not trying to catch the seller in lies (though that sometimes happens); you're trying to understand what you're really buying. Even honest sellers may not realize certain issues exist or may have normalized problems over years of ownership. Your job is to see the business with fresh eyes, verify the facts, and quantify the risks. The cost of thorough due diligence (₹1-3 lakh in professional fees) is trivial compared to the cost of buying a problematic business.

| Due Diligence Area | What You're Checking | Typical Duration |
|-------------------|---------------------|------------------|
| Financial | Revenue, profit, cash flow accuracy | 2-3 weeks |
| Legal | Compliance, contracts, litigation | 2-3 weeks |
| Tax | GST, income tax, PF/ESI compliance | 2-3 weeks |
| Operational | Systems, processes, quality | 1-2 weeks |
| Customer | Revenue quality, relationships | 1-2 weeks |
| Employee | HR compliance, key person risk | 1-2 weeks |

---

## Why Is Due Diligence Critical for Business Buyers?

**Due diligence protects you from overpaying for a business, inheriting hidden liabilities, and discovering operational problems after it's too late to adjust price or walk away.** Skipping or rushing due diligence is the most common mistake first-time buyers make.

The asymmetry of information in business sales works against buyers. Sellers know everything about their business; you know only what they choose to share. Due diligence levels that playing field. Consider what you're checking for: unreported debt, pending lawsuits, tax non-compliance, customer concentration, key employee flight risk, equipment problems, lease issues, and dozens of other potential deal-killers. Each issue discovered is an opportunity to adjust price, negotiate protections, or walk away from a bad deal. Issues discovered after closing are expensive problems you now own.

**Cost of Incomplete Due Diligence:**

| Risk Not Discovered | Potential Cost |
|--------------------|----------------|
| Undisclosed tax liability | ₹10-50 lakh+ in back taxes, penalties |
| Pending litigation | Unknown damages, legal fees |
| Customer concentration (key customer leaving) | 20-50% revenue loss |
| Equipment needing replacement | ₹5-30 lakh capital requirement |
| Lease not transferable | Business must relocate |
| Key employee departing | Operations disruption, knowledge loss |
| Environmental liability | Cleanup costs, regulatory fines |
| PF/ESI non-compliance | Back payments plus penalties |

## What Financial Records Should You Review?

**Review at least 3-5 years of income tax returns, GST filings, bank statements, audited financials (if available), and detailed accounts receivable/payable aging to verify reported revenue and profitability.** Cross-reference documents to identify discrepancies.

Financial due diligence is the foundation. Start by understanding how money flows through the business: how are sales generated, invoiced, and collected? How are expenses incurred and paid? Map this to bank statements and verify that reported figures match reality. In Indian SMBs, pay special attention to cash transactions, related-party transactions, and owner personal expenses run through the business. All of these affect true profitability and must be normalized for accurate valuation.

**Financial Due Diligence Checklist:**

1. **Income Tax Returns (3-5 years)**
   - [ ] ITR forms with computation
   - [ ] Compare reported income to financial statements
   - [ ] Note any assessments or disputes with IT department

2. **GST Returns (Since implementation)**
   - [ ] GSTR-1 (outward supplies)
   - [ ] GSTR-3B (summary returns)
   - [ ] Cross-reference with bank deposits
   - [ ] Check for any notices or demands

3. **Bank Statements (3 years, all accounts)**
   - [ ] Verify deposits match reported revenue
   - [ ] Review payment patterns
   - [ ] Identify unusual transactions
   - [ ] List all accounts including FDs

4. **Profit & Loss Statements**
   - [ ] Monthly statements for past 2-3 years
   - [ ] Revenue trends by month/quarter
   - [ ] Gross margin analysis
   - [ ] SG&A breakdown

5. **Balance Sheets**
   - [ ] Asset verification (physical inspection)
   - [ ] Liability completeness
   - [ ] Working capital analysis
   - [ ] Equity and retained earnings

6. **Accounts Receivable**
   - [ ] Aging report (current/30/60/90+ days)
   - [ ] Verify major receivables exist
   - [ ] Collection history by customer
   - [ ] Bad debt write-offs

7. **Accounts Payable**
   - [ ] Aging report
   - [ ] Verify no undisclosed debts
   - [ ] Critical vendor payment status
   - [ ] Trade credit terms

8. **Debt Schedule**
   - [ ] All loans and credit facilities
   - [ ] Outstanding balances
   - [ ] Repayment terms
   - [ ] Collateral pledged

| Document | Why It Matters | Red Flag |
|----------|---------------|----------|
| Bank statements | Shows actual cash flows | Deposits << reported revenue |
| GST returns | Independent revenue verification | Significant discrepancies |
| ITR | Legal record of income | Major variance from books |
| AR aging | Quality of receivables | >30% over 90 days |
| AP aging | Hidden liabilities | Critical vendors past due |

## What Legal Documents Must Be Verified?

**Legal due diligence covers business registration, all licenses and permits, property rights, contracts and agreements, intellectual property, and any pending or potential litigation.** Engage a lawyer experienced in M&A for this review.

Legal issues can kill deals or create massive post-closing liabilities. Verify that the business entity exists legally and has been properly maintained. Confirm all required licenses and permits are current and transferable (or can be reissued to you). Review every significant contract to understand assignment provisions, change-of-control clauses, and termination rights. Check for any litigation, arbitration, or regulatory proceedings. Title to all assets must be clear and transferable.

**Legal Due Diligence Checklist:**

1. **Corporate Documents**
   - [ ] Certificate of incorporation
   - [ ] Memorandum of Association (MOA)
   - [ ] Articles of Association (AOA)
   - [ ] All board resolutions
   - [ ] Shareholder agreements
   - [ ] Partnership deed (if applicable)

2. **Licenses and Permits**
   - [ ] Shop and Establishment license
   - [ ] Trade license from local authority
   - [ ] FSSAI license (if food-related)
   - [ ] Pollution/environmental clearances
   - [ ] Factory license (if applicable)
   - [ ] Industry-specific licenses
   - [ ] Verify all are current and transferable

3. **Property and Lease**
   - [ ] Property ownership documents (if owned)
   - [ ] Lease agreements (if leased)
   - [ ] Lease transferability provisions
   - [ ] Remaining lease term
   - [ ] Rent escalation clauses
   - [ ] Security deposits

4. **Contracts and Agreements**
   - [ ] Customer contracts
   - [ ] Supplier agreements
   - [ ] Employment contracts
   - [ ] Non-compete agreements
   - [ ] Franchise agreements (if applicable)
   - [ ] Change-of-control provisions

5. **Intellectual Property**
   - [ ] Trademark registrations
   - [ ] Domain name ownership
   - [ ] Patents (if any)
   - [ ] Copyright ownership
   - [ ] Trade secrets documentation

6. **Litigation and Disputes**
   - [ ] Pending lawsuits
   - [ ] Past litigation history
   - [ ] Threatened claims
   - [ ] Arbitration proceedings
   - [ ] Regulatory investigations

| License/Permit | Typical Business Types | Transfer Process |
|---------------|----------------------|------------------|
| Shop & Establishment | All retail/commercial | New application by buyer |
| Trade License | All businesses | Municipal transfer process |
| FSSAI | Food, restaurants, F&B | Transfer application |
| Pollution/PCB | Manufacturing | State PCB process |
| Factory License | Manufacturing | Labor department process |
| Fire NOC | Commercial premises | Typically with property |

## What Tax Compliance Must Be Checked?

**Verify compliance with Goods and Services Tax (GST), Income Tax, Employee Provident Fund (EPF), Employees' State Insurance (ESI), and Professional Tax to avoid inheriting liabilities and penalties.** Tax non-compliance is common in Indian SMBs and can result in significant exposure.

Tax due diligence in India requires specific attention to GST (relatively new, many businesses still have compliance gaps), income tax (assess any pending assessments or disputes), and employee-related statutory contributions. Outstanding tax liabilities typically remain with the business entity in share purchases—you inherit them. Even in asset purchases, certain liabilities (like property tax) may follow the assets. Structure indemnification provisions in the purchase agreement to protect against undisclosed tax exposure.

**Tax Due Diligence Checklist:**

1. **GST Compliance**
   - [ ] GST registration certificate
   - [ ] All returns filed (GSTR-1, 3B)
   - [ ] Payment challans
   - [ ] Input tax credit reconciliation
   - [ ] Any notices or demands
   - [ ] E-way bill compliance (if applicable)

2. **Income Tax**
   - [ ] ITR filed for all years
   - [ ] TDS compliance (employer and contractor)
   - [ ] Any ongoing assessment
   - [ ] Pending demands or appeals
   - [ ] Advance tax payments
   - [ ] Tax audit reports (if applicable)

3. **Employee Statutory Compliance**
   - [ ] EPF registration and payments
   - [ ] ESI registration and payments
   - [ ] Labour Welfare Fund contributions
   - [ ] Professional Tax deductions
   - [ ] Verify contributions for all employees

4. **Local and Other Taxes**
   - [ ] Property tax (if property owned)
   - [ ] Municipal taxes
   - [ ] Entry tax/octroi (legacy issues)
   - [ ] Industry-specific levies

| Tax Type | Typical Non-Compliance Issues | Risk Level |
|----------|------------------------------|------------|
| GST | Late filing, input credit mismatches | High (penalties steep) |
| Income Tax | Underreported income, TDS defaults | High |
| EPF | Non-registration, underpayment | High (employee claims) |
| ESI | Non-coverage of eligible employees | Medium-High |
| Professional Tax | Non-deduction | Low-Medium |

## How Do You Verify Customer and Revenue Quality?

**Review customer concentration, revenue retention rates, contract terms, and whenever possible, speak directly with major customers to verify relationships and understand their intentions post-acquisition.** Revenue quality often matters more than revenue quantity.

Not all revenue is equal. Recurring revenue from contracted customers is worth more than one-time project revenue. Diversified revenue across many customers is less risky than concentration in a few. Long-tenured customer relationships that predate current ownership are more likely to survive transition than recent wins that may depend on personal relationships. Request customer-level revenue data for at least two years to understand concentration, trends, and churn.

**Customer Due Diligence Checklist:**

1. **Revenue Analysis**
   - [ ] Customer-level revenue (top 20 minimum)
   - [ ] Revenue concentration (% from top 1, 5, 10 customers)
   - [ ] Revenue trend by customer
   - [ ] New vs repeat customer revenue
   - [ ] Contract vs non-contract revenue

2. **Customer Relationships**
   - [ ] Relationship tenure (years with business)
   - [ ] Contract terms and expiration dates
   - [ ] Payment terms and history
   - [ ] Relationship held by whom (owner vs company)

3. **Customer Conversations**
   - [ ] Speak with 5-10 major customers
   - [ ] Understand relationship strength
   - [ ] Ask about service satisfaction
   - [ ] Gauge reaction to ownership change
   - [ ] Assess likelihood of continuity

| Customer Metric | Low Risk | Medium Risk | High Risk |
|-----------------|----------|-------------|-----------|
| Top customer % | <10% | 10-25% | >25% |
| Top 5 customers % | <35% | 35-50% | >50% |
| Contract coverage | >70% contracted | 40-70% | <40% |
| Average tenure | >5 years | 2-5 years | <2 years |
| Relationship | With company | Mixed | Owner-dependent |

## What Operational Issues Should Be Examined?

**Operational due diligence covers systems and processes, equipment condition, inventory accuracy, key vendor relationships, quality metrics, and the overall ability of the business to function without the current owner.** This reveals how well the business can transition to new ownership.

Walk through the business physically. Observe operations during working hours. Review documented processes (if they exist). Test equipment and assess condition. Count inventory and compare to records. Speak with employees about how things really work. Understand the daily rhythm of the business. This hands-on review reveals things documents cannot: cultural issues, equipment problems, workflow bottlenecks, and employee concerns. Ask yourself: could I run this business tomorrow if the seller disappeared?

**Operational Due Diligence Checklist:**

1. **Systems and Processes**
   - [ ] Standard operating procedures documented?
   - [ ] Accounting system and financial controls
   - [ ] Customer management/CRM
   - [ ] Inventory management system
   - [ ] Quality control processes
   - [ ] IT systems and data security

2. **Equipment and Assets**
   - [ ] Complete equipment list
   - [ ] Physical verification and condition
   - [ ] Maintenance records
   - [ ] Remaining useful life estimate
   - [ ] Replacement cost budget

3. **Inventory**
   - [ ] Physical inventory count
   - [ ] Compare to book values
   - [ ] Identify obsolete/slow-moving items
   - [ ] Supplier and ordering processes

4. **Vendor Relationships**
   - [ ] Key supplier list and terms
   - [ ] Contract transferability
   - [ ] Pricing and payment terms
   - [ ] Backup supplier options

5. **Quality and Customer Service**
   - [ ] Customer complaint history
   - [ ] Return/refund rates
   - [ ] Quality metrics if tracked
   - [ ] Online reviews and reputation

## What Employee and HR Issues Need Review?

**Review employee contracts, compensation structure, statutory compliance (EPF, ESI), key person retention risk, and organizational culture to understand workforce stability and potential liabilities.** Employees often determine acquisition success or failure.

Employees carry institutional knowledge that may not be documented. Their reaction to ownership change significantly impacts transition success. Verify that all statutory compliances are met—outstanding EPF or ESI liabilities can be substantial. Identify key employees whose departure would damage the business and plan retention strategies. Understand compensation levels compared to market—significantly below-market pay may indicate flight risk; above-market creates cost pressure.

**Employee Due Diligence Checklist:**

1. **Employment Records**
   - [ ] Complete employee list with tenure
   - [ ] Employment contracts (or appointment letters)
   - [ ] Job descriptions and reporting structure
   - [ ] Performance reviews if available

2. **Compensation**
   - [ ] Salary details for all employees
   - [ ] Benefits and allowances
   - [ ] Bonus and incentive structure
   - [ ] Comparison to market rates

3. **Statutory Compliance**
   - [ ] EPF registration and universal account numbers
   - [ ] Verify EPF payments for all eligible employees
   - [ ] ESI coverage verification
   - [ ] Gratuity provisions/policy
   - [ ] Leave records and encashment liability

4. **Key Person Risk**
   - [ ] Identify critical employees
   - [ ] Assess retention likelihood
   - [ ] Non-compete agreements in place?
   - [ ] Succession/backup for key roles

5. **Culture and Morale**
   - [ ] Turnover rates
   - [ ] Tenure distribution
   - [ ] Employee-owner relationships
   - [ ] Any outstanding disputes or grievances

| HR Item | What to Verify | Risk If Missing |
|---------|---------------|-----------------|
| EPF compliance | Universal Account Numbers, payment receipts | Back payments + penalties |
| ESI compliance | IP numbers, contribution records | Back payments + penalties |
| Employee contracts | Written agreements with key terms | Dispute risk |
| Gratuity provision | Policy and funding | Unfunded liability |
| Leave encashment | Records and provision | Unfunded liability |

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How long should due diligence take?

**Thorough due diligence for a small business acquisition typically takes 4-8 weeks, with the timeline depending on business complexity, seller preparedness, and document availability.** Simple businesses with clean records and cooperative sellers can complete due diligence in 3-4 weeks. Complex businesses with multiple locations, large employee bases, or extensive regulatory requirements may need 8-12 weeks. Resist pressure to rush—it's better to extend the timeline than skip verification. Include due diligence duration in your LOI and ensure exclusivity covers the full period plus buffer for final negotiations and documentation.

### How much does due diligence cost?

**Professional due diligence for a small business acquisition typically costs ₹75,000 to ₹3 lakh, covering CA fees for financial review, lawyer fees for legal examination, and possibly consultant fees for operational assessment.** For a straightforward acquisition under ₹50 lakh, budget ₹75,000-1.5 lakh. For ₹50 lakh to ₹2 crore deals, budget ₹1.5-3 lakh. Larger or complex transactions may require more extensive (and expensive) review. This cost is small compared to deal value and potential savings from issues discovered. Many buyers who skip professional due diligence to save money end up paying far more to fix problems they inherit.

### What are the biggest red flags in due diligence?

**The biggest red flags include major discrepancies between reported and verified revenue, undisclosed liabilities, pending litigation, key employee flight risk, and non-transferable critical assets or contracts.** Also watch for: seller unable or unwilling to provide requested documents, revenue heavily dependent on owner relationships, equipment in poor condition not matching representations, significant compliance gaps (tax, regulatory), and lease issues that could force relocation. One red flag may be manageable; multiple red flags suggest walking away or significant price adjustment. Trust your instincts—if something feels wrong, investigate further.

### What happens if I find problems during due diligence?

**Problems discovered during due diligence are opportunities to renegotiate price, adjust deal structure, require indemnification, or walk away from a bad deal.** Quantify each issue's financial impact. Present findings to the seller factually, not accusatorially. Propose solutions: price reduction for quantifiable issues, escrow holdbacks for uncertain liabilities, indemnification clauses for potential future claims, or earnout structures that shift risk. If the seller refuses reasonable adjustments for material issues, be willing to walk away. The best protection from a bad deal is not doing it. Your due diligence period exists specifically for this purpose.

### Should I do due diligence myself or hire professionals?

**For any acquisition above ₹25-30 lakh, engage professionals—a CA for financial due diligence and a lawyer for legal review—while still being actively involved yourself.** Professionals bring expertise, experience, and objectivity. They know what to look for and can spot issues you'd miss. However, don't outsource entirely. Participate in document review, join site visits, conduct customer conversations yourself, and understand every finding. You're making the investment decision, not your advisors. For smaller deals, you can do more yourself with professional consultation as needed, but never skip legal review of purchase documents regardless of deal size.
