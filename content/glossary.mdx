# Business Acquisition Glossary

Everything you need to understand when buying a business in India. This comprehensive glossary covers valuation methods, deal structures, legal requirements, and operational terms used in business acquisitions and M&A transactions.

---

## Valuation Terms

## What is SDE (Seller's Discretionary Earnings)?

Seller's Discretionary Earnings (SDE) represents the total financial benefit a single owner-operator derives from a business annually. SDE is calculated by taking net profit and adding back the owner's salary, personal expenses run through the business, one-time costs, and non-cash expenses like depreciation. In India, SDE is the primary valuation metric for small businesses valued under ₹10 crore, particularly proprietorships and partnership firms where owner involvement is substantial.

For example, a business showing ₹20 lakh net profit with ₹15 lakh owner salary, ₹3 lakh personal car expenses, and ₹2 lakh depreciation would have an SDE of ₹40 lakh. Buyers use SDE multiples (typically 1.5x-3.5x) to determine fair purchase price. SDE works best when the buyer plans to operate the business themselves.

**Related terms:** [EBITDA](#what-is-ebitda), [Multiple](#what-is-a-valuation-multiple), [Owner Benefit](#what-is-owner-benefit)

## What is EBITDA?

EBITDA stands for Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization. It measures a company's operational profitability independent of financing decisions, tax structures, and accounting methods. Unlike SDE, EBITDA does not add back owner compensation, making it the preferred metric for larger businesses where professional management runs operations without heavy owner involvement.

In India, EBITDA is standard for mid-market transactions (₹10 crore+) and private equity deals. A manufacturing company with ₹2 crore net profit, ₹30 lakh interest payments, ₹50 lakh taxes, and ₹40 lakh depreciation would report EBITDA of ₹3.2 crore. EBITDA multiples in India typically range from 3x-8x depending on industry, growth rate, and market conditions. Buyers should verify EBITDA is calculated using consistent accounting standards (Ind AS or previous GAAP).

**Related terms:** [SDE](#what-is-sde-sellers-discretionary-earnings), [Adjusted EBITDA](#what-is-adjusted-ebitda), [Multiple](#what-is-a-valuation-multiple)

## What is Adjusted EBITDA?

Adjusted EBITDA takes standard EBITDA and normalizes it by removing non-recurring, unusual, or non-operational items that don't reflect ongoing business performance. Common adjustments include one-time legal settlements, COVID-related anomalies, related-party transactions at non-market rates, and extraordinary expenses like office relocation costs. In India, adjustments often address family employment arrangements, personal expenses run through the company, and inconsistent accounting practices.

For instance, if a business paid the owner's brother ₹25 lakh above market salary, adjusted EBITDA would add back that excess. Similarly, one-time GST penalties or pandemic-era losses would be normalized. Adjusted EBITDA provides a clearer picture of sustainable earnings, which is critical for accurate valuation. Buyers should scrutinize every adjustment and require supporting documentation—aggressive adjustments can artificially inflate purchase price.

**Related terms:** [EBITDA](#what-is-ebitda), [Normalization](#what-is-normalization-in-business-valuation), [Quality of Earnings](#what-is-a-quality-of-earnings-report)

## What is a Valuation Multiple?

A valuation multiple is a ratio used to determine business value by multiplying a financial metric (typically SDE or EBITDA) by an industry-appropriate factor. Small businesses in India typically sell for 1.5x-3.5x SDE, while mid-market companies command 3x-8x EBITDA. The multiple reflects risk, growth potential, market conditions, and business quality. A stable manufacturing business with diverse customers might sell at 4x EBITDA, while a fast-growing SaaS company could command 8x or higher.

Factors increasing multiples include: recurring revenue, strong growth trajectory, proprietary technology, and low customer concentration. Factors decreasing multiples include: owner dependency, declining revenues, regulatory risks, and limited documentation. In India, multiples tend to be lower than Western markets due to higher perceived risk and less developed acquisition financing options. Always compare multiples against recent comparable transactions in your specific industry.

**Related terms:** [SDE](#what-is-sde-sellers-discretionary-earnings), [EBITDA](#what-is-ebitda), [Comparable Transaction Analysis](#what-is-comparable-transaction-analysis)

## What is DCF (Discounted Cash Flow)?

Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) is a valuation method that estimates business value based on projected future cash flows, discounted back to present value using an appropriate discount rate. DCF accounts for the time value of money—₹100 received today is worth more than ₹100 received in five years. The discount rate typically ranges from 15-30% for Indian private companies, reflecting risk and required returns.

DCF involves projecting 5-10 years of future cash flows, calculating a terminal value, and discounting everything to present value. While theoretically robust, DCF relies heavily on assumptions about future growth, margins, and discount rates. Small changes in assumptions dramatically affect valuation. In India, DCF is common in PE/VC deals and larger transactions but less practical for small business acquisitions where historical earnings provide better guidance. Most buyers use DCF as a sanity check alongside multiple-based valuations.

**Related terms:** [Terminal Value](#what-is-terminal-value), [WACC](#what-is-wacc-weighted-average-cost-of-capital), [Fair Market Value](#what-is-fair-market-value)

## What is Asset Valuation?

Asset valuation determines business worth by calculating the fair market value of all assets minus liabilities. This method is particularly relevant for asset-heavy businesses like manufacturing, real estate, or equipment-dependent operations. Assets valued include: machinery, inventory, real estate, vehicles, intellectual property, receivables, and cash. In India, registered valuers certified by IBBI (Insolvency and Bankruptcy Board of India) conduct formal asset valuations for regulatory compliance.

Asset valuation establishes a floor price—a business should rarely sell below its asset value unless in distress. For example, a printing business with ₹2 crore in machinery, ₹50 lakh inventory, and ₹30 lakh receivables has minimum asset value of ₹2.8 crore (less liabilities). Going concern value typically exceeds asset value when the business generates healthy cash flows. Asset-based valuation is critical when structuring [asset purchases](#what-is-an-asset-purchase) versus [share purchases](#what-is-a-share-purchase).

**Related terms:** [Book Value](#what-is-book-value), [Fair Market Value](#what-is-fair-market-value), [Liquidation Value](#what-is-liquidation-value)

## What is Goodwill?

Goodwill represents the intangible value of a business beyond its physical assets—including brand reputation, customer relationships, trained workforce, proprietary processes, and market position. When a buyer pays ₹5 crore for a business with ₹3 crore in net tangible assets, the ₹2 crore premium represents goodwill. In India, goodwill is recognized as an asset on the balance sheet following acquisitions and must be tested annually for impairment under Ind AS 36.

From a tax perspective, goodwill created through acquisition can be depreciated at 25% per year under the Income Tax Act (Section 32), though recent amendments have restricted this benefit. Buyers should understand that goodwill often diminishes if key employees leave, customer relationships weaken, or the seller's reputation was the primary value driver. Protecting goodwill through [non-compete agreements](#what-is-a-non-compete-agreement) and proper [transition periods](#what-is-a-transition-period) is essential.

**Related terms:** [Intangible Assets](#what-is-an-intangible-asset), [Asset Valuation](#what-is-asset-valuation), [Non-Compete](#what-is-a-non-compete-agreement)

## What is Book Value?

Book value is the net worth of a company as recorded in its financial statements—total assets minus total liabilities. For a private limited company, this equals the share capital plus accumulated reserves and surplus. Book value represents historical accounting values, not current market values, which can differ significantly. A piece of land purchased for ₹50 lakh in 2005 might have a book value of ₹50 lakh but a market value of ₹5 crore.

In Indian acquisitions, book value provides a baseline for negotiation but rarely reflects true business worth. Growing businesses typically command premiums above book value (price-to-book ratios of 2x-10x), while struggling businesses may sell below book value. Book value is most relevant for holding companies, real estate businesses, or NBFCs where asset values dominate. Buyers should always conduct independent valuations rather than relying solely on book value, as accounting treatments can obscure or inflate actual asset worth.

**Related terms:** [Asset Valuation](#what-is-asset-valuation), [Fair Market Value](#what-is-fair-market-value), [Net Worth](#what-is-net-worth)

## What is Fair Market Value?

Fair Market Value (FMV) is the price at which a business would change hands between a willing buyer and willing seller, both having reasonable knowledge of relevant facts, and neither under compulsion to transact. FMV is a legal and regulatory standard used for tax purposes, related-party transactions, and statutory compliance in India. The Income Tax Act requires FMV determination for share transfers, ESOPs, and amalgamations.

SEBI-registered merchant bankers or IBBI-registered valuers typically provide FMV certificates. For private company shares, FMV is calculated using methods prescribed under Rule 11UA of Income Tax Rules—either Net Asset Value (NAV) or DCF method. Transactions below FMV can trigger tax consequences for both parties. For unlisted shares, FMV also determines capital gains liability. Buyers should obtain FMV certificates before closing to ensure compliance and avoid future tax disputes.

**Related terms:** [DCF](#what-is-dcf-discounted-cash-flow), [Book Value](#what-is-book-value), [Registered Valuer](#what-is-a-registered-valuer)

## What is Owner Benefit?

Owner benefit encompasses all financial advantages an owner receives from a business, including salary, dividends, perquisites, personal expenses, and distributions. It's similar to SDE but sometimes calculated more broadly. Owner benefit helps buyers understand the true economic value of owning the business beyond reported profits. In India, where tax planning often minimizes reported income, owner benefit analysis reveals actual cash flows.

A business owner might draw a modest ₹12 lakh salary while the company pays for their car (₹8 lakh), house rent (₹6 lakh), travel (₹5 lakh), and family phone bills (₹1 lakh). Combined with ₹20 lakh in dividends and ₹15 lakh net profit, the total owner benefit is ₹67 lakh—far exceeding the reported salary. Understanding owner benefit helps buyers structure appropriate offers and plan post-acquisition compensation. This metric is essential for evaluating lifestyle businesses and proprietorships.

**Related terms:** [SDE](#what-is-sde-sellers-discretionary-earnings), [EBITDA](#what-is-ebitda), [Normalization](#what-is-normalization-in-business-valuation)

## What is Terminal Value?

Terminal value represents the estimated worth of a business beyond the explicit forecast period in a DCF valuation—typically capturing value from year 6 onward into perpetuity. Terminal value often comprises 60-80% of total DCF value, making its calculation critical. Two methods are common: the Gordon Growth Model (assuming perpetual growth rate) and Exit Multiple Method (applying an EBITDA multiple to final year earnings).

For Indian businesses, terminal value calculations must account for higher discount rates reflecting emerging market risk. A conservative perpetual growth rate of 4-6% (nominal, accounting for inflation) is typical. Using the Gordon Growth formula: Terminal Value = Final Year Cash Flow × (1 + Growth Rate) ÷ (Discount Rate - Growth Rate). If discount rate is 18% and growth rate is 5%, the multiplier is approximately 8x final year cash flow. Buyers should stress-test terminal value assumptions, as small changes dramatically impact valuation.

**Related terms:** [DCF](#what-is-dcf-discounted-cash-flow), [Discount Rate](#what-is-a-discount-rate), [WACC](#what-is-wacc-weighted-average-cost-of-capital)

## What is Comparable Transaction Analysis?

Comparable transaction analysis values a business by examining prices paid in similar recent acquisitions. Analysts identify transactions in the same industry, size range, and geography, then calculate implied multiples (EV/EBITDA, EV/Revenue) to apply to the target company. In India, finding truly comparable transactions is challenging due to limited deal disclosure and market fragmentation, but databases like VCCEdge, Venture Intelligence, and news aggregators help.

For example, if three digital marketing agencies sold recently at 4x, 4.5x, and 5x EBITDA, a similar agency might expect 4-5x EBITDA valuation. Adjustments are made for size (larger businesses command premiums), growth rates, profitability, and market conditions. The method is most useful for mid-market transactions where comparable data exists. Buyers should compile 5-10 comparable transactions and understand why each sold at its specific multiple. Industry brokers and M&A advisors often have proprietary transaction data.

**Related terms:** [Multiple](#what-is-a-valuation-multiple), [EBITDA](#what-is-ebitda), [Enterprise Value](#what-is-enterprise-value)

## What is Enterprise Value?

Enterprise Value (EV) represents the total value of a business, including both equity and debt. It's calculated as: Equity Value + Total Debt - Cash and Cash Equivalents. EV provides an ownership-neutral valuation, allowing comparison between companies with different capital structures. When someone says a business is "worth ₹10 crore," they might mean equity value or enterprise value—the distinction matters significantly.

In Indian M&A, purchase price is typically quoted as enterprise value, with adjustments at closing for actual cash and debt levels. A company with ₹10 crore EV, ₹2 crore debt, and ₹50 lakh cash would have equity value of ₹8.5 crore (what shareholders receive). EV/EBITDA multiples are standard for comparison because they normalize for capital structure differences. Buyers should always clarify whether discussions reference EV or equity value to avoid misunderstandings that could cost crores.

**Related terms:** [EBITDA](#what-is-ebitda), [Multiple](#what-is-a-valuation-multiple), [Equity Value](#what-is-equity-value)

## What is a Registered Valuer?

A Registered Valuer in India is a professional certified by the Insolvency and Bankruptcy Board of India (IBBI) to conduct business valuations for statutory purposes. Following the Companies Act 2013 amendments and IBC 2016, registered valuers are mandatory for specific transactions: mergers, demergers, share buybacks, and insolvency proceedings. Valuers must pass examinations, complete educational requirements, and maintain membership with a Registered Valuers Organisation (RVO).

There are three valuation asset classes: Land & Building, Plant & Machinery, and Securities or Financial Assets. Business valuations fall under Securities or Financial Assets. While IBBI-registered valuers are mandatory for statutory compliance, any qualified professional can provide valuation advisory for private transactions. However, using registered valuers adds credibility and regulatory protection. Buyers should verify valuer credentials on IBBI's website and ensure independence from the seller to avoid conflicts of interest.

**Related terms:** [Fair Market Value](#what-is-fair-market-value), [IBBI](#what-is-ibbi), [Valuation Report](#what-is-a-valuation-report)

## Deal Structure Terms

## What is an LOI (Letter of Intent)?

A Letter of Intent (LOI) is a preliminary document outlining the principal terms of a proposed acquisition before detailed negotiations and due diligence begin. LOIs typically include proposed purchase price, deal structure, key conditions, exclusivity period, and timeline. In India, most LOI provisions are non-binding except for exclusivity, confidentiality, and governing law clauses, which are explicitly made binding.

An LOI signals serious buyer intent and secures exclusive negotiating rights (typically 60-90 days). During this exclusivity period, the seller cannot negotiate with other buyers. Key LOI elements include: purchase price and structure, what's included in the sale, conditions precedent, due diligence scope, and expected closing date. While non-binding, renegotiating LOI terms later damages trust and often kills deals. Buyers should conduct preliminary due diligence before signing LOIs to ensure terms are realistic. A well-drafted LOI accelerates deal completion.

**Related terms:** [SPA](#what-is-an-spa-share-purchase-agreement), [Due Diligence](#what-is-due-diligence), [Exclusivity](#what-is-exclusivity-in-ma)

## What is an SPA (Share Purchase Agreement)?

A Share Purchase Agreement (SPA) is the definitive legal contract governing the sale of company shares from seller to buyer. It's the primary transaction document for [share purchases](#what-is-a-share-purchase), containing all negotiated terms: purchase price, representations and warranties, indemnification provisions, closing conditions, and post-closing obligations. Indian SPAs are governed by the Indian Contract Act 1872 and must comply with Companies Act 2013 requirements.

Key SPA sections include: definitions, purchase price mechanism (including adjustments), representations and warranties (extensive promises about business condition), covenants (what each party must do), conditions to closing, indemnification (who pays for breaches), and termination rights. SPAs for Indian transactions also address stamp duty optimization, capital gains tax treatment, and regulatory filings. Typical SPA negotiation takes 4-8 weeks after LOI signing. Both parties should engage experienced M&A lawyers—SPA terms have significant financial implications for years post-closing.

**Related terms:** [LOI](#what-is-an-loi-letter-of-intent), [Representations & Warranties](#what-is-representations-and-warranties), [APA](#what-is-an-apa-asset-purchase-agreement)

## What is an APA (Asset Purchase Agreement)?

An Asset Purchase Agreement (APA) governs the sale of specific business assets rather than company shares. Unlike SPAs, where buyers acquire the entire company (including liabilities), APAs allow selective acquisition of desired assets while leaving unwanted liabilities behind. In India, APAs are common for proprietorships, partnership firms, and when buyers want to avoid historical tax or legal issues associated with the selling entity.

Typical APA contents include: detailed asset schedule (equipment, inventory, contracts, IP, goodwill), excluded assets, assumed versus excluded liabilities, purchase price allocation (important for tax treatment), representations, and transition provisions. Assets often require individual transfer mechanisms—vehicle transfers through RTO, property through registration, contracts through novation agreements. APAs tend to be more complex operationally but cleaner legally. Buyers should ensure customer contracts and key licenses can actually be transferred, as some require consent or aren't transferable at all.

**Related terms:** [Asset Purchase](#what-is-an-asset-purchase), [Share Purchase](#what-is-a-share-purchase), [SPA](#what-is-an-spa-share-purchase-agreement)

## What is an Asset Purchase?

An asset purchase is an acquisition structure where the buyer purchases specific business assets (equipment, inventory, contracts, IP, goodwill) rather than acquiring company shares. The selling entity remains intact; only the assets change hands. Asset purchases are the standard structure for acquiring proprietorships and partnership firms in India, as these entities cannot sell "shares." For private limited companies, asset purchases are chosen strategically.

Advantages of asset purchases include: avoiding unknown liabilities, cherry-picking desired assets, potential tax benefits through stepped-up asset basis, and avoiding corporate history issues. Disadvantages include: complexity of transferring individual assets, possible loss of contracts requiring counterparty consent, fresh license applications, and higher stamp duty in some states. In India, asset purchases are common for distressed acquisitions, acquiring business divisions, or when the seller has significant tax disputes. Buyers should map every asset requiring transfer and timeline appropriately.

**Related terms:** [APA](#what-is-an-apa-asset-purchase-agreement), [Share Purchase](#what-is-a-share-purchase), [Goodwill](#what-is-goodwill)

## What is a Share Purchase?

A share purchase is an acquisition structure where the buyer acquires all (or majority) shares of the target company, becoming the new owner of the entire legal entity. The company continues operating with the same PAN, bank accounts, contracts, and licenses—only ownership changes. Share purchases are the standard structure for acquiring private limited companies in India when the buyer wants business continuity.

Advantages include: simplified transfer (no individual asset transfers needed), automatic assumption of contracts and licenses, business continuity, and often lower stamp duty than asset purchases. Disadvantages include: inheriting all liabilities (known and unknown), potential tax history issues, and legacy legal matters. Due diligence is critical for share purchases since buyers assume complete responsibility for corporate history. Share transfers require board approvals, share transfer forms (SH-4), and ROC filings. Long-term capital gains tax applies to sellers; buyers should ensure proper tax withholding and obtain necessary certificates.

**Related terms:** [SPA](#what-is-an-spa-share-purchase-agreement), [Asset Purchase](#what-is-an-asset-purchase), [Due Diligence](#what-is-due-diligence)

## What is an Earnout?

An earnout is a contingent payment mechanism where a portion of the purchase price is paid over time based on the business achieving specified performance targets post-acquisition. Earnouts bridge valuation gaps when buyers and sellers disagree on future potential. The seller might receive ₹4 crore upfront plus up to ₹2 crore over two years if revenue targets are met, aligning total payment with actual performance.

Earnouts are particularly relevant for Indian businesses in high-growth sectors or those dependent on seller relationships. Common metrics include revenue, EBITDA, customer retention, or specific milestones. Structure matters significantly: earnouts can be fixed payments (₹50 lakh if target met) or sliding scales (percentage of excess revenue). Sellers dislike earnouts because they shift risk post-sale; buyers appreciate the protection. Clear definitions, measurement methodology, and dispute resolution mechanisms are essential. Earnouts often require sellers to remain involved during the earnout period, creating complex governance arrangements.

**Related terms:** [Seller Financing](#what-is-seller-financing), [Holdback](#what-is-a-holdback), [Transition Period](#what-is-a-transition-period)

## What is Seller Financing?

Seller financing occurs when the business seller provides a loan to the buyer as part of the purchase price, rather than requiring full cash payment at closing. The buyer pays a portion upfront (typically 50-70%) and finances the remainder through the seller via a promissory note with agreed interest rate and repayment schedule. Seller financing is common in Indian SME acquisitions where bank financing is difficult to obtain.

Advantages for buyers: easier deal financing, seller remains invested in business success, demonstrates seller confidence in the business. Advantages for sellers: can command higher purchase price, earns interest income, installment capital gains tax treatment may apply. Typical terms: 15-20% interest rate, 2-4 year repayment period, personal guarantees from buyer, and business assets as security. In India, seller notes must comply with RBI regulations on lending between private parties. Seller financing typically represents 20-40% of purchase price and should be documented through formal loan agreements with clear default provisions.

**Related terms:** [Seller Note](#what-is-a-seller-note), [Earnout](#what-is-an-earnout), [Down Payment](#what-is-a-down-payment)

## What is a Holdback?

A holdback is a portion of the purchase price retained by the buyer for a specified period after closing to secure seller obligations—particularly indemnification claims for representations and warranties breaches. Holdbacks typically range from 10-20% of purchase price and are held for 12-24 months post-closing. If no claims materialize, the holdback releases to the seller; otherwise, buyers recover damages directly from the holdback.

In Indian transactions, holdbacks serve as practical protection given the difficulty of pursuing post-closing claims through litigation. Without holdbacks, buyers must chase sellers for indemnification payments—often unsuccessfully. Holdbacks can be held by buyers directly or placed in escrow with a neutral third party. Key negotiation points include: holdback amount, duration, release mechanism (automatic or requiring buyer consent), and which representations the holdback covers. Sellers prefer smaller holdbacks with shorter durations; buyers prefer larger amounts held longer. Finding middle ground is part of standard deal negotiation.

**Related terms:** [Escrow](#what-is-escrow-in-ma), [Indemnification](#what-is-indemnification), [Representations & Warranties](#what-is-representations-and-warranties)

## What is Escrow in M&A?

Escrow is an arrangement where a neutral third party (escrow agent) holds funds or documents on behalf of transaction parties, releasing them only when specified conditions are satisfied. In M&A, escrow commonly holds: purchase price pending closing, holdback amounts, and key documents. Escrow provides security to both parties—sellers know funds exist, buyers know funds won't release until conditions are met.

In India, law firms, banks, or specialized escrow services act as escrow agents. Escrow agreements specify: what's held, release conditions, timeline, agent responsibilities, and fee allocation. For cross-border deals or larger transactions, international escrow services add credibility. Common escrow arrangements include: paying purchase price into escrow before closing (releases upon document execution), and holding indemnification reserves post-closing. Costs typically range from 0.1-0.5% of escrowed amount. While not universal in Indian SME deals, escrow increasingly appears in mid-market transactions as buyers and sellers recognize mutual protection benefits.

**Related terms:** [Holdback](#what-is-a-holdback), [Closing](#what-is-closing-in-ma), [Purchase Price](#what-is-purchase-price)

## What is Working Capital Adjustment?

Working capital adjustment is a purchase price mechanism ensuring the business maintains adequate operating liquidity at closing. The SPA defines a target working capital level (typically based on historical averages); post-closing, actual working capital is measured and the purchase price adjusted accordingly. If working capital exceeds target, buyers pay more; if below, sellers refund the difference.

Working capital generally includes: cash, accounts receivable, inventory, and prepaid expenses, minus accounts payable, accrued expenses, and short-term liabilities. For example, if target working capital is ₹50 lakh and actual closing working capital is ₹35 lakh, the buyer receives ₹15 lakh adjustment from the seller. This mechanism prevents sellers from "stripping" the business by collecting receivables early, delaying payables, or reducing inventory before closing. Indian SME deals often use simplified net working capital calculations. Detailed working capital schedules and measurement methodologies should be negotiated during SPA drafting to prevent post-closing disputes.

**Related terms:** [Closing](#what-is-closing-in-ma), [SPA](#what-is-an-spa-share-purchase-agreement), [Net Working Capital](#what-is-net-working-capital)

## What is Exclusivity in M&A?

Exclusivity (also called "no-shop" provision) is a contractual commitment where the seller agrees not to solicit, negotiate, or accept offers from other potential buyers for a specified period while negotiating with the exclusive buyer. Exclusivity periods typically range from 60-120 days in Indian transactions, providing buyers confidence to invest time and money in due diligence without competitive interference.

Exclusivity appears in LOIs and is one of the few binding provisions in otherwise non-binding preliminary agreements. It protects buyers who may spend ₹5-25 lakh on due diligence (legal, financial, technical) from being used as a "stalking horse" to generate competing bids. Sellers grant exclusivity to serious buyers demonstrating capability and intent. If deals fall through during exclusivity, sellers must wait until the period expires before approaching other buyers. Buyers should negotiate adequate exclusivity periods for thorough due diligence; rushing due diligence to meet artificial deadlines leads to missed issues and deal regret.

**Related terms:** [LOI](#what-is-an-loi-letter-of-intent), [Due Diligence](#what-is-due-diligence), [Deal Process](#what-is-a-deal-process)

## What is a Break Fee?

A break fee (or termination fee) is a payment one party makes to the other if the transaction fails to close due to specified reasons—typically the paying party walking away or failing to satisfy closing conditions. Break fees compensate the non-breaching party for time, expenses, and lost opportunity. While common in large public company M&A, break fees are less frequent in Indian SME transactions but appear increasingly in competitive situations.

Typical break fees range from 1-3% of transaction value. Seller break fees protect buyers who've invested in due diligence if sellers accept a competing bid. Buyer break fees (reverse break fees) protect sellers if buyers fail to secure financing or simply walk away. In India, break fees must be reasonable to be enforceable—courts may view excessive fees as penalties rather than liquidated damages. Break fees should cover documented expenses and reasonable opportunity cost. Negotiating break fees signals serious intent from both parties.

**Related terms:** [LOI](#what-is-an-loi-letter-of-intent), [Exclusivity](#what-is-exclusivity-in-ma), [Closing](#what-is-closing-in-ma)

## What is Closing in M&A?

Closing (also called completion) is the formal event when ownership transfers from seller to buyer—signing final documents, paying purchase price, and delivering shares or assets. In Indian transactions, closing typically involves executing the SPA, share transfer forms (SH-4), board resolutions, payment confirmation, and handover of business materials. Post-closing, the buyer assumes control and begins operating the business.

Closings can be simultaneous (signing and closing occur together) or split (signing occurs first, closing follows after satisfying conditions). Split closings are common when regulatory approvals, landlord consents, or financing conditions require time after signing. Indian closings also involve: stamp duty payment (varying by state—typically 0.25% on shares), ROC filings within prescribed timelines, and director resignations/appointments. Pre-closing checklists ensure nothing is missed: document execution, payment mechanisms, key deliveries, and access transfers. Working with experienced transaction lawyers ensures smooth closings that satisfy all legal requirements.

**Related terms:** [SPA](#what-is-an-spa-share-purchase-agreement), [Working Capital Adjustment](#what-is-working-capital-adjustment), [Conditions Precedent](#what-is-conditions-precedent)

## What is Conditions Precedent?

Conditions precedent (CPs) are requirements that must be satisfied before parties are obligated to close a transaction. CPs give parties exit rights if specified conditions cannot be met, protecting against closing transactions that shouldn't proceed. Common CPs include: satisfactory due diligence completion, regulatory approvals obtained, no material adverse change, financing secured, and landlord consents received.

In Indian SPAs, conditions precedent typically include: no regulatory objections, key employee agreements signed, certain representations remaining true, no pending litigation affecting the business, and sometimes third-party consents. Each CP should specify who's responsible for satisfying it, timeline for satisfaction, and consequences of non-satisfaction. Buyers should ensure adequate CP protection for items outside their control; sellers should minimize open-ended CPs that give buyers easy exits. The waiver mechanism (allowing parties to proceed despite unmet CPs) should be clearly documented. Sophisticated deals include CP certificates confirming satisfaction.

**Related terms:** [Closing](#what-is-closing-in-ma), [Material Adverse Change](#what-is-material-adverse-change-mac), [Due Diligence](#what-is-due-diligence)

## Due Diligence Terms

## What is Due Diligence?

Due diligence is the comprehensive investigation a buyer conducts to verify all aspects of a target business before completing an acquisition. It covers financial, legal, tax, operational, commercial, and technical dimensions. Due diligence confirms information provided during negotiations, identifies risks and liabilities, and validates valuation assumptions. In Indian transactions, due diligence typically takes 30-60 days following LOI signing.

Financial due diligence examines books of accounts, revenue quality, expense patterns, and working capital. Legal due diligence reviews contracts, litigation, compliance, and corporate records. Tax due diligence assesses GST, income tax, TDS compliance, and transfer pricing. Operational due diligence covers customers, suppliers, employees, and processes. Buyers assemble due diligence teams of accountants, lawyers, and industry experts. Findings are documented in due diligence reports highlighting risks, deal-breakers, price adjustment factors, and items requiring SPA protection. Skipping due diligence to "save money" is the most expensive mistake in acquisitions.

**Related terms:** [Data Room](#what-is-a-data-room), [Red Flags](#what-are-due-diligence-red-flags), [Quality of Earnings](#what-is-a-quality-of-earnings-report)

## What is Representations and Warranties?

Representations and warranties (reps and warranties) are statements of fact made by the seller (and sometimes buyer) in the SPA, covering business condition, legal compliance, financial accuracy, and other material matters. Representations are statements about existing facts; warranties are promises those facts are true. If representations prove false, the other party has recourse through indemnification claims.

Common seller representations in Indian SPAs include: financial statements are accurate, no undisclosed liabilities, tax compliance is current, contracts are valid and enforceable, no pending litigation, proper employee compliance (PF/ESI/gratuity), and no environmental issues. Representations are heavily negotiated—sellers prefer qualified statements ("to seller's knowledge"), while buyers want absolute representations. Materiality thresholds, disclosure schedules, and survival periods (how long reps remain claimable) are key negotiation points. Strong representations with meaningful indemnification backing are buyers' primary protection against discovering post-closing problems. Never accept weak reps to accelerate closing.

**Related terms:** [Indemnification](#what-is-indemnification), [Disclosure Schedule](#what-is-a-disclosure-schedule), [SPA](#what-is-an-spa-share-purchase-agreement)

---

## What is Indemnification?

Indemnification is the contractual mechanism obligating one party (typically the seller) to compensate the other party (buyer) for losses arising from breached representations, warranties, or covenants. It's the buyer's remedy when post-closing problems emerge that should have been the seller's responsibility. Indemnification provisions include: scope of covered claims, survival periods, claim procedures, caps, baskets, and payment mechanisms.

Key indemnification terms in Indian SPAs: survival period (how long after closing claims can be made—typically 18-36 months for general representations, longer for tax and fundamental representations); basket/deductible (minimum loss threshold before claims are valid—protects against nuisance claims); cap (maximum total indemnification—often 10-30% of purchase price); and escrow/holdback (funds secured for indemnification payment). Specific indemnities for known issues (pending tax cases, litigation, environmental matters) often have separate carve-outs from standard caps. Buyers should negotiate robust indemnification tied to meaningful seller assets or holdbacks.

**Related terms:** [Representations & Warranties](#what-is-representations-and-warranties), [Holdback](#what-is-a-holdback), [Escrow](#what-is-escrow-in-ma)

## What is Material Adverse Change (MAC)?

A Material Adverse Change (MAC) clause allows a party to terminate the transaction if significant negative changes affect the target business between signing and closing. MAC clauses protect buyers who signed deals based on certain business conditions from being forced to close when those conditions materially deteriorate. Defining what constitutes "material" is heavily negotiated and often the most contentious SPA provision.

Standard MAC definitions exclude: general economic conditions, industry-wide changes, pandemic effects (post-COVID), changes in law, and effects of the announced transaction itself. What's typically included: significant customer losses, key employee departures, regulatory issues affecting the specific business, and material financial deterioration. In India, MAC clauses have limited judicial interpretation, making clear contractual definitions essential. Quantitative thresholds (e.g., revenue decline exceeding 20%) provide more certainty than purely subjective standards. Sellers want narrow MAC definitions; buyers want broad protection. Finding balance requires understanding each party's genuine concerns.

**Related terms:** [Conditions Precedent](#what-is-conditions-precedent), [SPA](#what-is-an-spa-share-purchase-agreement), [Closing](#what-is-closing-in-ma)

## What is a Disclosure Schedule?

A disclosure schedule is an exhibit to the SPA listing exceptions to the seller's representations and warranties. Rather than representing "no litigation," the seller represents "no litigation except as disclosed in Schedule X," which then lists any pending matters. Disclosure schedules bring known issues into the open, preventing future indemnification claims based on disclosed matters while maintaining clean representations for undisclosed items.

Effective disclosure schedules in Indian transactions typically cover: pending legal cases, known tax disputes, material contracts, related party arrangements, exceptions to compliance representations, and specific employee matters. Disclosure schedules should be specific and detailed—vague disclosures may not provide adequate protection. Buyers should review schedules carefully during due diligence, as disclosed items don't generate indemnification claims. Sellers sometimes over-disclose defensively; buyers should distinguish genuine issues from defensive disclosure. The negotiation often involves removing items from schedules (increasing seller exposure) or adding indemnification for specifically disclosed problems.

**Related terms:** [Representations & Warranties](#what-is-representations-and-warranties), [Indemnification](#what-is-indemnification), [Due Diligence](#what-is-due-diligence)

## What is a Quality of Earnings Report?

A Quality of Earnings (QofE) report is an in-depth financial due diligence analysis, typically prepared by accounting firms, that validates EBITDA, identifies adjustments, and assesses earnings quality and sustainability. QofE goes beyond audited financials to examine revenue recognition, expense timing, customer concentration, one-time items, and working capital patterns. It's the gold standard for financial due diligence in mid-market transactions.

QofE reports in India typically analyze 2-3 years of financials plus recent interim periods. Key areas examined: revenue sustainability (recurring vs. non-recurring), customer and supplier dependencies, related party transactions, adjustment legitimacy, working capital trends, and forward projections reasonableness. QofE often reveals that seller's "adjusted EBITDA" includes aggressive adjustments that don't survive scrutiny. Reports cost ₹5-25 lakh depending on business complexity and are worth every rupee for transactions above ₹5 crore. Lenders also require QofE for acquisition financing. Findings frequently result in purchase price adjustments.

**Related terms:** [Adjusted EBITDA](#what-is-adjusted-ebitda), [Due Diligence](#what-is-due-diligence), [Normalization](#what-is-normalization-in-business-valuation)

## What is a Data Room?

A data room is a secure repository containing documents and information for buyer due diligence review. Modern data rooms are virtual (VDR)—cloud-based platforms with access controls, document watermarking, and activity tracking. Sellers populate data rooms with financial records, legal documents, contracts, compliance certificates, and other materials requested by buyers. Organized data rooms accelerate due diligence and signal seller professionalism.

Standard data room organization for Indian transactions includes: corporate documents (incorporation, shareholder agreements, board minutes), financial statements and tax returns, material contracts, employee records, compliance certificates (GST, PF, ESI, Professional Tax), property documents, IP registrations, and litigation files. Popular VDR providers include Intralinks, Datasite, and DropBox Business. Sophisticated sellers prepare data rooms before marketing the business, dramatically improving process efficiency. Buyers should request complete due diligence checklists upfront and track document requests versus deliveries. Missing documents often indicate underlying issues.

**Related terms:** [Due Diligence](#what-is-due-diligence), [Confidentiality Agreement](#what-is-a-confidentiality-agreement), [Information Memorandum](#what-is-an-information-memorandum)

## What are Due Diligence Red Flags?

Due diligence red flags are warning signs discovered during investigation that indicate potential problems, increased risk, or need for deeper analysis. Red flags don't necessarily kill deals but require explanation, additional diligence, price adjustments, or enhanced contractual protections. Experienced buyers develop pattern recognition for common red flags across transaction types.

Common red flags in Indian acquisitions include: inconsistencies between GST returns and financials, significant related party transactions, high customer concentration (one customer >30% revenue), key person dependency, declining revenues with optimistic projections, unusual accounting policies, missing compliance documentation, frequent auditor changes, unexplained cash transactions, significant pending litigation, and seller reluctance to provide information. Environmental issues, labor disputes, and pending regulatory matters are serious red flags requiring specialist assessment. Each red flag should be documented with business response and risk assessment. Major red flags warrant walking away; minor flags inform negotiation and structuring.

## India-Specific Legal Terms

## What is ROC (Registrar of Companies)?

The Registrar of Companies (ROC) is the government authority under the Ministry of Corporate Affairs responsible for registering companies, maintaining corporate records, and ensuring Companies Act compliance. Each Indian state has designated ROCs where companies file incorporation documents, annual returns, financial statements, and event-based filings (director changes, share transfers, charge registrations). ROC records are publicly searchable through the MCA portal.

During acquisitions, buyers verify ROC filings to confirm: company incorporation status, director history, charge registrations (secured loans), annual compliance, share capital structure, and filed financial statements. Inconsistencies between claimed company history and ROC records signal compliance issues or misrepresentation. Post-acquisition filings include: share transfer forms (SH-4), director appointment/resignation forms (DIR-12), and beneficial ownership declarations. Stamp duty on share transfers varies by state and must be paid before ROC accepts filings. Companies with outstanding ROC compliance issues face penalties and restrictions—buyers should clear compliance before closing or escrow funds for regularization.

**Related terms:** [MCA](#what-is-mca-ministry-of-corporate-affairs), [Share Transfer Form](#what-is-a-share-transfer-form-sh-4), [Annual Return](#what-is-an-annual-return)

## What is MCA (Ministry of Corporate Affairs)?

The Ministry of Corporate Affairs (MCA) is the Indian government ministry responsible for administering the Companies Act 2013, Limited Liability Partnership Act 2008, and related corporate law. MCA oversees company registration, corporate governance standards, and regulatory compliance through ROCs across India. The MCA21 portal (mca.gov.in) is the primary interface for all company filings, fee payments, and public record searches.

For acquisitions, MCA resources enable: company verification through Master Data, financial statement access, director identification number (DIN) verification, charge searches, and compliance status review. Key MCA filings during acquisitions include MGT-14 (board resolutions), SH-4 (share transfers), DIR-12 (director changes), and CHG-1/4 (charge registration/satisfaction). Recent MCA initiatives relevant to acquisitions include the Company Fresh Start Scheme (CFSS) for compliance regularization and V3 portal improvements. Buyers should ensure target companies maintain active MCA status—"struck off" or "dormant" status creates complications. Professional company secretaries typically handle MCA compliance and filings.

**Related terms:** [ROC](#what-is-roc-registrar-of-companies), [Companies Act](#what-is-the-companies-act-2013), [DIN](#what-is-din-director-identification-number)

## What is GST Due Diligence?

GST due diligence examines a target company's Goods and Services Tax compliance, identifying unpaid liabilities, input credit irregularities, and potential future demands. GST became India's unified indirect tax from July 2017, replacing multiple central and state levies. Comprehensive GST review is essential for any acquisition since GST non-compliance can result in substantial demands, interest, and penalties that survive ownership changes in share purchases.

Key GST due diligence areas include: GSTR-1 (outward supplies) and GSTR-3B (summary returns) reconciliation, input tax credit (ITC) eligibility and matching, e-way bill compliance, HSN code accuracy, reverse charge mechanism compliance, and pending assessments or notices. Common issues include: unclaimed ITC due to supplier non-compliance, late filing penalties, and misclassification of goods/services. Buyers should obtain GST compliance certificates and representations with specific indemnification for undisclosed liabilities. GST portal access and CA-verified reconciliations are standard due diligence requests. Material GST exposures warrant purchase price adjustments or specific escrows.

**Related terms:** [Due Diligence](#what-is-due-diligence), [TDS](#what-is-tds-tax-deducted-at-source), [Indirect Tax](#what-is-indirect-tax)

## What is TDS (Tax Deducted at Source)?

Tax Deducted at Source (TDS) is India's withholding tax mechanism where the payer deducts tax before making payments to payees and remits it to the government. TDS applies to salaries, contractor payments, rent, interest, professional fees, and numerous other payment categories. TDS compliance is critical in acquisitions because buyers assume liability for unremitted TDS in share purchases, and TDS default can result in disallowance of business expenses.

Key TDS considerations in acquisitions: verify TDS compliance for all applicable payments, check TAN registration and quarterly return filings (24Q, 26Q), confirm Form 16/16A issuance, and review any notices or demands. TDS also applies to the transaction itself: share purchases trigger TDS under Section 194-IA for immovable property and Section 195 for payments to non-residents. Sellers must obtain Tax Clearance Certificate or provide undertakings for TDS matters. Common TDS issues include: delayed deposits (interest applicable from payment date), short deductions, and incorrect PAN/rate applications. TDS defaults have personal liability implications for directors.

**Related terms:** [GST Due Diligence](#what-is-gst-due-diligence), [Income Tax](#what-is-income-tax-due-diligence), [Tax Indemnity](#what-is-a-tax-indemnity)

## What is PF/ESI Compliance?

Provident Fund (PF) and Employee State Insurance (ESI) are statutory employee benefits administered by EPFO and ESIC respectively. PF requires employers to contribute 12% of employee wages to retirement accounts; ESI provides health insurance for employees earning below ₹21,000 monthly. Both schemes are mandatory for establishments meeting specified thresholds (20+ employees for PF, 10+ for ESI in most states). PF/ESI non-compliance is a significant liability in acquisitions.

Due diligence for PF/ESI covers: registration status, monthly contribution compliance, wage ceiling adherence, contractor PF coverage, settlement of past dues, and pending inspections or demands. Common issues include: excluding eligible employees, underreporting wages, not covering contract workers, and delayed remittances. PF/ESI defaults include interest, damages, and even criminal prosecution. In share purchases, buyers inherit all historical PF/ESI liabilities. Audited compliance certificates from the company's PF consultant, along with EPFO/ESIC portal verification, provide confirmation. Material exposures should be specifically indemnified or escrowed. PF/ESI cleanup before sale is strongly recommended.

**Related terms:** [Labor Due Diligence](#what-is-labor-due-diligence), [Employee Liabilities](#what-are-employee-liabilities), [Gratuity](#what-is-gratuity)

## What is NCLT (National Company Law Tribunal)?

The National Company Law Tribunal (NCLT) is India's specialized quasi-judicial body handling corporate law matters, including insolvency proceedings, mergers, demergers, and shareholder disputes. Established under the Companies Act 2013, NCLT replaced the Company Law Board and has exclusive jurisdiction over corporate restructuring. For acquisitions, NCLT involvement indicates either distressed situations (IBC proceedings) or formal restructuring requiring tribunal approval.

Mergers and demergers of private companies require NCLT approval, involving shareholder/creditor meetings and tribunal hearings. The NCLT process typically takes 6-12 months and involves legal costs of ₹5-15 lakh. Alternatively, Section 233 provides a faster "Fast Track Merger" route for small companies through Regional Directors without full NCLT process. Pending NCLT matters against a target company require careful due diligence—insolvency petitions, oppression/mismanagement cases, or winding-up applications significantly impact acquisitions. NCLT orders are publicly available and should be searched during due diligence.

**Related terms:** [IBC](#what-is-ibc-insolvency-and-bankruptcy-code), [Merger](#what-is-a-merger), [Demerger](#what-is-a-demerger)

## What is Stamp Duty on Share Transfer?

Stamp duty is a state-level tax payable when transferring shares, making it a significant transaction cost in Indian acquisitions. Stamp duty rates on share transfers vary by state, typically ranging from 0.25% (many states) to 0.75% (some states like Karnataka for unlisted shares) of share value. The transfer is legally valid only after stamp duty payment—unstamped instruments are inadmissible as evidence and attract penalties.

Since February 2019, stamp duty collection is centralized through the MCA portal for demat transfers, simplifying compliance. For physical share certificates (common in private companies), stamp duty is paid through physical franking or e-stamping before ROC filing. Stamp duty optimization strategies include: structuring deals as share purchases (typically lower duty than asset transfers), selecting buyer entity jurisdiction strategically, and ensuring arm's length valuations. Share transfer forms (SH-4) must be stamped within prescribed periods (typically 30 days). Late stamping attracts penalties up to 10x the original duty. Stamp duty should be factored into transaction cost calculations.

**Related terms:** [Share Transfer Form](#what-is-a-share-transfer-form-sh-4), [ROC](#what-is-roc-registrar-of-companies), [Transaction Costs](#what-are-transaction-costs)

## What is a Share Transfer Form (SH-4)?

SH-4 is the prescribed form under Companies Act 2013 for transferring shares between parties in private limited companies. The seller executes SH-4 as transferor; the buyer signs as transferee. SH-4 captures: share certificate details, number of shares, transfer price, and party information. Proper execution, stamping, and submission to the company's board enables registration of share transfer in company records.

Key requirements for valid SH-4 execution: both parties must sign, stamp duty must be paid (evidenced by franking or e-stamp), original share certificates must be submitted, and board must approve the transfer (private company shares typically have transfer restrictions). The company registers transfers within prescribed timelines and issues new share certificates to the buyer. Multiple SH-4 forms are needed if acquiring from multiple sellers. Post-transfer, ROC filing (MGT-14 for board resolution, and beneficial ownership forms if applicable) completes regulatory compliance. Ensure SH-4 consideration matches SPA purchase price to avoid valuation discrepancy issues. Template forms are available on the MCA website.

**Related terms:** [Stamp Duty](#what-is-stamp-duty-on-share-transfer), [ROC](#what-is-roc-registrar-of-companies), [Share Purchase](#what-is-a-share-purchase)

## What is FEMA Compliance?

The Foreign Exchange Management Act (FEMA) governs foreign exchange transactions and foreign investment in India. Any acquisition involving non-resident buyers, foreign-owned entities, or cross-border payments requires FEMA compliance. The Reserve Bank of India (RBI) administers FEMA, with investment provisions consolidated under Foreign Exchange Management (Non-debt Instruments) Rules 2019.

Key FEMA considerations for acquisitions: sector-specific FDI caps (e.g., insurance 74%, defense 74%, retail varies), approval routes (automatic vs. government approval), pricing guidelines for share transfers (fair market value floor), reporting requirements (FC-TRS forms), and downstream investment rules. Non-resident sellers must ensure sale proceeds are properly repatriated through authorized channels. Indian buyers acquiring companies with FDI history must verify past FEMA compliance. FEMA violations carry significant penalties—up to three times the contravention amount. Cross-border acquisitions require specialist legal advice to ensure proper structuring, pricing, and filings. Documentation includes bank certifications, CA certificates, and RBI filings.

**Related terms:** [FDI](#what-is-fdi-foreign-direct-investment), [RBI](#what-is-rbi-reserve-bank-of-india), [Cross-Border M&A](#what-is-cross-border-ma)

## What is IBC (Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code)?

The Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code 2016 (IBC) is India's comprehensive framework for resolving corporate insolvency through time-bound processes. IBC enables creditors to initiate insolvency proceedings against defaulting companies, potentially leading to resolution (acquisition) or liquidation. IBC has created a significant market for distressed acquisitions in India.

Under IBC's Corporate Insolvency Resolution Process (CIRP), Resolution Professionals take control of debtor companies, invite resolution plans (essentially acquisition bids), and seek creditor approval. Successful resolution applicants acquire companies free of historical liabilities through NCLT-approved plans. IBC acquisitions offer significant discounts (often 50-80% haircuts for creditors) but involve complex processes, creditor negotiations, and regulatory oversight. Eligibility requirements (Section 29A) disqualify certain buyers including willful defaulters and connected parties. IBC timelines theoretically span 330 days but often extend. Buyers considering IBC targets should engage IBC specialists and prepare for operational challenges during transition.

## Financing Terms

## What is a Seller Note?

A seller note is a formal loan from the business seller to the buyer, documenting the seller financing portion of an acquisition. Seller notes are legally binding promissory notes specifying principal amount, interest rate, repayment schedule, security arrangements, and default provisions. In Indian SME acquisitions, seller notes typically comprise 20-40% of purchase price when bank financing isn't available or buyers want to limit upfront cash requirements.

Key seller note terms include: interest rate (typically 12-18% annually in India), repayment period (2-5 years), amortization schedule (monthly/quarterly payments or bullet repayment), security (often subordinated to bank debt, secured by business assets or personal guarantees), and prepayment rights. Seller notes align seller and buyer interests post-closing—if the business fails, sellers lose their note value. Subordination to bank debt is common when buyers also use bank financing. Seller notes must comply with money lending regulations; proper documentation with legal counsel is essential. For sellers, notes provide installment sale treatment potentially deferring capital gains tax on the deferred portion.

## What is Acquisition Financing?

Acquisition financing encompasses all funding sources used to complete a business purchase. Common sources include: buyer's equity contribution, seller financing, bank loans, NBFC lending, private debt funds, and in larger deals, mezzanine financing and private equity. Indian SME acquisitions typically combine buyer equity (40-60%), seller financing (20-40%), and sometimes bank debt (20-40%) when available.

Bank acquisition financing in India remains challenging for SME deals—banks prefer lending against established borrower relationships rather than for acquisitions. NBFC products offer more flexibility but at higher interest rates (14-20%). Asset-based lending against receivables, inventory, or equipment can supplement acquisition financing. For larger deals (₹25 crore+), structured financing with multiple tranches becomes viable. Buyers should secure financing commitments before signing LOIs, as financing failure is a leading cause of broken deals. Key financing considerations include: debt service coverage (can the business cash flows service the debt?), personal guarantee requirements, security packages, and prepayment flexibility.

## What is Bank Financing for Acquisitions?

Bank financing for acquisitions involves obtaining loans from commercial banks to fund business purchases. In India, acquisition financing from banks is available primarily through: term loans against existing business cash flows, loans against property (LAP), and specific acquisition finance products from select banks like HDFC, ICICI, and some PSU banks. Bank financing typically covers 40-60% of acquisition cost, with buyers contributing remaining equity.

Requirements for bank acquisition financing include: profitable target business with 3+ years history, 1.5x+ debt service coverage ratio, collateral (often personal property), personal guarantees from buyers, detailed business plan, and strong buyer credentials (industry experience, net worth). Interest rates range from 10-14% depending on borrower profile and collateral. Processing takes 4-8 weeks; buyers should start early. Banks require comprehensive due diligence reports and may commission independent valuations. For acquisitions of smaller businesses (under ₹5 crore), bank financing is difficult to obtain—seller financing typically fills the gap. Relationships with business bankers are valuable for acquisition financing access.

**Related terms:** [Acquisition Financing](#what-is-acquisition-financing), [DSCR](#what-is-dscr-debt-service-coverage-ratio), [Collateral](#what-is-collateral)

## What is Mezzanine Financing?

Mezzanine financing is a hybrid debt-equity instrument that sits between senior debt and pure equity in the capital structure. It carries higher interest rates than senior debt (18-24% in India) because it's subordinate to bank loans but senior to equity, with higher risk requiring higher returns. Mezzanine often includes equity conversion features (warrants) giving lenders upside participation.

In Indian M&A, mezzanine financing is relevant for mid-market transactions (₹25 crore+) where bank debt alone is insufficient. Mezzanine providers include specialized funds, family offices, and alternative investment platforms. Typical mezzanine terms: subordinated to senior debt, limited or no collateral, cash interest plus PIK (payment-in-kind) interest, 4-7 year maturity, and equity warrants for 5-15% ownership. Mezzanine enables higher leverage (increasing buyer returns) but increases repayment burden. Covenants are typically less restrictive than bank debt. Due diligence by mezzanine providers is thorough, similar to equity investors. Mezzanine is particularly useful when acquisition targets have strong cash flows but limited hard assets for bank collateral.

**Related terms:** [Acquisition Financing](#what-is-acquisition-financing), [Subordination](#what-is-subordination), [Private Equity](#what-is-private-equity)

## What is DSCR (Debt Service Coverage Ratio)?

Debt Service Coverage Ratio (DSCR) measures a business's ability to service debt obligations from operating cash flows. DSCR is calculated as: Operating Cash Flow (or EBITDA) ÷ Total Debt Service (principal + interest payments). A DSCR of 1.5x means the business generates 50% more cash than needed for debt payments—providing cushion for variability. Lenders typically require minimum 1.25x-1.5x DSCR for acquisition financing.

DSCR is critical for structuring acquisition financing. Example: a business with ₹1.5 crore EBITDA can support approximately ₹75 lakh annual debt service at 2x DSCR coverage. This might translate to ₹2.5-3 crore debt capacity depending on interest rates and loan tenure. Buyers should model post-acquisition DSCR including all new debt (acquisition loans, working capital, existing debt). DSCR below 1.0x indicates inability to service debt from operations—a red flag for lenders and buyers. Seasonal businesses may have periods of low DSCR; annual DSCR should remain healthy. Banks monitor DSCR quarterly and covenant breaches can trigger loan acceleration.

**Related terms:** [Acquisition Financing](#what-is-acquisition-financing), [Bank Financing](#what-is-bank-financing-for-acquisitions), [EBITDA](#what-is-ebitda)

## What is a Down Payment?

A down payment is the initial cash portion a buyer pays from personal/available funds when purchasing a business, excluding borrowed amounts. In acquisition financing, the down payment represents the buyer's equity investment. Typical down payments for Indian SME acquisitions range from 30-60% of purchase price, depending on seller flexibility and available financing. Higher down payments reduce debt burden and demonstrate buyer commitment.

Down payment requirements vary by transaction: cash-flow-strong businesses with multiple interested buyers command higher down payments; distressed or hard-to-sell businesses accept lower down payments with more seller financing. For buyers, balancing down payment against leverage is strategic—lower down payments preserve cash but increase debt service and interest costs. Sellers prefer higher down payments for immediate liquidity and reduced buyer default risk. Down payments are typically held in escrow pending closing or paid directly at closing. Proof of funds (bank statements, commitment letters) for down payments is standard LOI requirement. Insufficient down payment capacity disqualifies many potential buyers.

**Related terms:** [Acquisition Financing](#what-is-acquisition-financing), [Seller Financing](#what-is-seller-financing), [Escrow](#what-is-escrow-in-ma)

## Operational Terms

## What is Customer Concentration?

Customer concentration measures how dependent a business is on its largest customers. High concentration exists when a single customer represents more than 10-15% of revenue, or when the top 5 customers represent more than 40-50% of revenue. Customer concentration significantly impacts business risk and therefore valuation—losing a concentrated customer could devastate the business. Buyers discount values for high concentration.

Example: a business with 60% revenue from one customer trades at lower multiples than similar businesses with diversified customer bases. Key concentration due diligence includes: understanding customer relationships, reviewing contract terms and renewal histories, assessing customer health, and evaluating switching costs. Mitigating factors include: long-term contracts, high switching costs, and strong relationships with multiple contacts within customer organizations. Customer interviews during due diligence provide crucial insight. Post-acquisition, diversifying concentrated customer bases is a priority. SPAs often include specific representations about customer relationships and revenue breakdowns. Material customer loss between signing and closing can trigger MAC provisions.

**Related terms:** [Due Diligence](#what-is-due-diligence), [Revenue Quality](#what-is-revenue-quality), [Key Person Risk](#what-is-key-person-risk)

## What is Key Person Risk?

Key person risk refers to business dependence on specific individuals whose departure would significantly impact operations, customer relationships, or institutional knowledge. Common key persons include: founders, lead salespeople, technical experts, and managers with deep customer relationships. High key person risk reduces business value and requires mitigation through transaction structuring and transition planning.

Key person due diligence examines: who holds critical relationships and knowledge, what's their post-acquisition plan, are there successors in place, and can processes be documented and transferred? Mitigation strategies include: requiring key persons to sign employment agreements with the buyer, structuring earnouts tied to key person retention, negotiating non-compete and non-solicitation agreements, and planning extended transition periods. Key person life insurance can protect against unexpected departures. Businesses heavily dependent on the selling owner face highest key person risk—buyers should realistically assess whether the business can thrive without the founder. Reducing key person risk pre-sale significantly increases business value and buyer interest.

**Related terms:** [Non-Compete Agreement](#what-is-a-non-compete-agreement), [Transition Period](#what-is-a-transition-period), [Customer Concentration](#what-is-customer-concentration)

## What is a Non-Compete Agreement?

A non-compete agreement (also called restrictive covenant) prevents the seller from starting or joining a competing business for a specified period and geographic area after selling their business. Non-competes protect buyers from sellers using their industry knowledge, customer relationships, and reputation to compete against the business they just sold. Non-competes are standard in business acquisitions.

Indian courts enforce reasonable non-competes connected to business sales (unlike employment non-competes which face enforceability challenges). Reasonable terms typically include: 2-4 year duration, geographically limited to relevant markets, and scope limited to directly competitive activities. Overly broad non-competes risk unenforceability. Non-competes often include non-solicitation provisions (preventing sellers from hiring employees or soliciting customers). Earnout and seller financing structures provide additional practical protection—sellers competing against the business risk losing their deferred payments. Non-competes should be standalone agreements signed at closing, with consideration clearly documented. Violation remedies typically include injunctions and damages.

**Related terms:** [Key Person Risk](#what-is-key-person-risk), [Transition Period](#what-is-a-transition-period), [Goodwill](#what-is-goodwill)

## What is a Transition Period?

A transition period is the post-closing timeframe during which the seller assists the buyer in taking over business operations. Transition periods typically range from 30 days to 6 months, depending on business complexity and buyer experience. Transition support includes: customer introductions, supplier relationship transfers, employee orientation, system training, and general knowledge transfer. Transition periods are critical for successful acquisitions.

Transition terms should be clearly documented in the SPA or separate transition services agreement (TSA), specifying: duration, seller's time commitment (full-time vs. part-time), specific responsibilities, compensation (often included in purchase price or nominal salary), and escalation procedures. Common transition activities include: joint customer meetings, operations training, vendor introductions, and issue resolution. Extended transitions work well for complex businesses; shorter transitions suit simpler operations with experienced buyers. Earnout structures naturally extend seller involvement beyond formal transition periods. Buyers should plan transition carefully and avoid becoming dependent on sellers—the goal is knowledge transfer, not ongoing reliance.

**Related terms:** [Training Period](#what-is-a-training-period), [Key Person Risk](#what-is-key-person-risk), [Non-Compete](#what-is-a-non-compete-agreement)

## What is a Training Period?

A training period is a structured portion of the transition where the seller actively trains the buyer and their team on business operations, systems, and processes. Training is more intensive and focused than general transition support, often involving formal sessions, documentation creation, and hands-on learning. Adequate training is essential for buyers without industry experience or those unfamiliar with specific business systems.

Training period terms include: specific topics to be covered (operations, finance, sales, systems), hours of training provided, format (on-site vs. remote, documented vs. informal), timeline, and delivery responsibility. Common training topics for Indian SME acquisitions include: accounting systems (Tally, Zoho Books), customer relationship management, production processes, vendor management, employee supervision, and compliance procedures. Sellers should document key processes and procedures during training—this documentation survives the training period. Buyers should actively participate and take notes; training periods pass quickly. Training agreements often specify additional consulting availability (paid hourly) after formal training ends, ensuring buyers can access seller expertise for troubleshooting.

**Related terms:** [Transition Period](#what-is-a-transition-period), [Knowledge Transfer](#what-is-knowledge-transfer), [Standard Operating Procedures](#what-are-standard-operating-procedures-sops)

## What is Normalization in Business Valuation?

Normalization is the process of adjusting financial statements to reflect true ongoing operating performance, removing one-time items, personal expenses, and anomalies. Normalized financials provide accurate EBITDA or SDE figures for valuation purposes. Without normalization, historical financials may significantly understate or overstate actual business performance, leading to incorrect valuations.

Common normalizing adjustments include: adding back owner's above-market salary or excessive perks, removing one-time expenses (legal settlements, COVID impacts, relocation), adjusting related party transactions to market rates, removing personal expenses run through the business (travel, vehicles, phones), and adjusting for accounting policy changes. In India, normalization often addresses: family member employment at non-market rates, personal vehicle and property expenses, and discretionary expenses that new owners wouldn't incur. Each adjustment should be documented with supporting evidence. Aggressive normalization is common in seller presentations—buyers must scrutinize every adjustment and may reject or reduce unsupported claims. Quality of earnings reports provide independent normalization analysis.

**Related terms:** [Adjusted EBITDA](#what-is-adjusted-ebitda), [SDE](#what-is-sde-sellers-discretionary-earnings), [Quality of Earnings](#what-is-a-quality-of-earnings-report)

## What is Revenue Quality?

Revenue quality assesses how sustainable, predictable, and valuable a company's revenue streams are. High-quality revenue is recurring, diversified across customers, growing, and contracted. Low-quality revenue is one-time, concentrated, declining, or at-risk. Revenue quality significantly impacts valuation—businesses with recurring subscription revenue command higher multiples than project-based businesses with similar revenue levels.

Revenue quality due diligence examines: recurring vs. non-recurring mix, customer retention and churn rates, contract terms and renewal patterns, revenue growth trajectory, pricing power, and customer concentration. A SaaS business with 90% recurring revenue and 95% annual retention has far higher revenue quality than a consulting firm with project-based engagements. Buyers should analyze revenue by type, customer, and product to understand the underlying quality. Adjustments for revenue quality affect valuation multiples—a 3x multiple for project revenue might be 5x for comparable recurring revenue. Revenue quality also impacts financing availability; lenders prefer predictable cash flows for debt service.

**Related terms:** [Customer Concentration](#what-is-customer-concentration), [EBITDA](#what-is-ebitda), [Due Diligence](#what-is-due-diligence)

## What is an Information Memorandum?

An Information Memorandum (IM), also called Confidential Information Memorandum (CIM), is a comprehensive document prepared by sellers or their advisors to present the business to potential buyers. IMs typically include: business overview, products/services, market analysis, financial performance, growth opportunities, management team, and investment highlights. IMs are shared after buyers sign confidentiality agreements and express serious interest.

Well-prepared IMs for Indian acquisitions typically run 20-50 pages and include: executive summary, company history, business model description, competitive positioning, 3-5 years of financial statements, customer and supplier overviews (anonymized), employee summary, asset listings, and growth opportunities. IMs aim to generate buyer interest while providing sufficient information for preliminary valuation. They do NOT replace due diligence—IMs present seller's perspective and may be optimistic. Buyers should verify all IM claims during due diligence. Sellers without IMs appear unprepared; professional IMs signal serious sale intent and competent advisory support. Investment banks and M&A advisors typically prepare IMs for mid-market transactions.

**Related terms:** [Data Room](#what-is-a-data-room), [Due Diligence](#what-is-due-diligence), [Teaser](#what-is-a-deal-teaser)

## What is a Confidentiality Agreement?

A Confidentiality Agreement (also Non-Disclosure Agreement or NDA) is a legally binding contract requiring parties to keep shared information confidential. In acquisitions, sellers require buyers to sign NDAs before sharing sensitive business information. NDAs protect sellers from information misuse if deals don't close and from competitors posing as buyers to gather intelligence.

Standard acquisition NDA terms include: definition of confidential information, permitted uses (evaluation only), non-disclosure obligations, exclusions (publicly available information), term (typically 2-3 years), return/destruction of information, and non-solicitation of employees. Mutual NDAs protect both parties, though seller information flows more extensively. NDAs should prohibit buyers from contacting customers, suppliers, or employees without seller consent. Breach remedies typically include injunctive relief and damages, though proving damages from confidentiality breaches is difficult. Sophisticated sellers track NDA signatories and information access. Buyers should honor NDAs even when deals fail—reputation matters in acquisition communities.

**Related terms:** [Information Memorandum](#what-is-an-information-memorandum), [Due Diligence](#what-is-due-diligence), [Data Room](#what-is-a-data-room)

## Additional Essential Terms

## What is Equity Value?

Equity value represents the value attributable to shareholders—what equity holders would receive if the company were sold and all debts paid. Equity value equals Enterprise Value minus net debt (total debt minus cash). When purchasing shares in an acquisition, the buyer pays equity value, which reflects both business operations value and the capital structure. Equity value is what changes hands in a share purchase transaction.

The relationship between EV and equity value is crucial: a company with ₹10 crore EV, ₹3 crore debt, and ₹50 lakh cash has equity value of ₹7.5 crore. Buyers purchasing 100% of shares pay ₹7.5 crore to shareholders. Understanding this distinction prevents costly misunderstandings—when sellers quote "₹10 crore" value, clarify whether that's EV or equity. Purchase price negotiations typically start with EV, with equity value calculated based on closing date debt and cash levels. Post-closing working capital and debt adjustments refine the final equity consideration. Equity value is also called "offer price" or "purchase consideration" in transaction documents.

**Related terms:** [Enterprise Value](#what-is-enterprise-value), [Net Debt](#what-is-net-debt), [Purchase Price](#what-is-purchase-price)

---

## What is Net Debt?

Net debt is total debt minus cash and cash equivalents, representing the net liability position to be repaid. Net debt is a key component in bridging enterprise value to equity value. A company with ₹5 crore debt and ₹1 crore cash has ₹4 crore net debt. In acquisition pricing: Equity Value = Enterprise Value - Net Debt.

Debt definitions in M&A are carefully negotiated and typically include: bank loans, shareholder loans, bonds, finance leases, contingent liabilities, underfunded pension obligations, and unpaid dividends. Cash definitions include: bank balances, liquid investments, and sometimes restricted cash. Trapped cash in foreign subsidiaries or regulatory reserves may be excluded. Debt-free/cash-free deals are common—sellers retain cash, but buyers assume no debt. The SPA specifies exact definitions; post-closing true-ups adjust for actual closing debt and cash levels versus estimates. Material discrepancies between estimated and actual net debt trigger purchase price adjustments. Buyers should understand all debt and debt-like items during due diligence.

**Related terms:** [Enterprise Value](#what-is-enterprise-value), [Equity Value](#what-is-equity-value), [Working Capital](#what-is-working-capital-adjustment)

---

## What is Liquidation Value?

Liquidation value is the estimated amount realized if a business were closed and all assets sold individually, minus liabilities. It represents the floor value—what's recoverable in a worst-case shutdown scenario. Liquidation value is typically lower than going concern value (value as an operating business) because assets sold in liquidation fetch less than in normal transactions, and intangibles like goodwill realize nothing.

Liquidation analysis distinguishes orderly liquidation (time to find buyers, typically 60-80% of fair market value) from forced liquidation (quick sale, typically 40-60% of fair market value). Liquidation value is particularly relevant for: distressed acquisitions, asset-heavy businesses, and establishing negotiation floors. A manufacturing business might have ₹3 crore liquidation value but ₹5 crore going concern value—the ₹2 crore premium reflects operating business value creation. Buyers of struggling businesses should know liquidation value as downside protection. Banks use liquidation value for collateral assessment. IBC resolution plans must exceed liquidation value to gain creditor approval.

**Related terms:** [Asset Valuation](#what-is-asset-valuation), [Book Value](#what-is-book-value), [IBC](#what-is-ibc-insolvency-and-bankruptcy-code)

---

## What is Net Working Capital?

Net Working Capital (NWC) represents the capital required to fund day-to-day operations, calculated as current assets (receivables, inventory, prepaid expenses) minus current liabilities (payables, accrued expenses). Positive NWC means the business needs to maintain capital investment in operations; negative NWC (common in cash businesses) means operations generate capital.

NWC is critical in acquisition pricing because it determines how much operating capital the business needs. SPAs include working capital adjustment mechanisms specifying target NWC (typically historical average), with post-closing true-ups adjusting purchase price for deviations. If NWC is ₹20 lakh below target, buyers receive a refund; if ₹20 lakh above, sellers receive additional payment. NWC definitions are heavily negotiated—what's included/excluded, how items are valued, and measurement methodology. Common disputes arise over receivable collectibility, inventory valuation, and liability completeness. Buyers should conduct detailed NWC analysis during due diligence and model expected NWC at closing to avoid surprises.

**Related terms:** [Working Capital Adjustment](#what-is-working-capital-adjustment), [Due Diligence](#what-is-due-diligence), [Closing](#what-is-closing-in-ma)

## What is an Intangible Asset?

Intangible assets are non-physical assets that provide economic value to a business. Common intangibles include: trademarks and brand names, patents and proprietary technology, copyrights, customer relationships, supplier contracts, trained workforce, trade secrets, and software. Intangibles often comprise significant portions of business value, particularly for service, technology, and brand-driven companies.

In Indian acquisitions, key intangible due diligence covers: trademark registrations with the Controller General of Patents (IP India), patent validity and protection scope, software licensing compliance, domain name ownership, and customer contract transferability. Accounting treatment under Ind AS 38 requires intangible assets to be identifiable, controlled, and provide future economic benefits. Post-acquisition, intangibles are valued and recorded on the buyer's books—the excess of purchase price over tangible assets typically reflects intangible value and goodwill. Intangibles with definite lives are amortized; indefinite-life intangibles face annual impairment testing. Protecting acquired intangibles requires registration maintenance, confidentiality protection, and competitive vigilance.

**Related terms:** [Goodwill](#what-is-goodwill), [IP Due Diligence](#what-is-ip-due-diligence), [Asset Valuation](#what-is-asset-valuation)

## What is a Distressed Acquisition?

A distressed acquisition involves purchasing a business facing financial difficulties—typically unable to meet debt obligations, experiencing severe cash flow problems, or undergoing insolvency proceedings. Distressed acquisitions offer significant discounts (often 30-70% below fair value) but involve higher risk, complexity, and often operational turnaround requirements. India's IBC has created a structured market for distressed acquisitions.

Distressed acquisition routes include: direct negotiation with stressed companies, IBC resolution processes, bank-led sales of NPA accounts, and liquidation auctions. Each route has different timelines, pricing dynamics, and legal implications. IBC acquisitions provide liability protection through NCLT approval; direct purchases may inherit legacy issues. Key distressed acquisition considerations: understanding why the business failed, whether underlying issues are fixable, working capital needs for stabilization, employee and stakeholder management, and integration challenges. Distressed specialists bring turnaround expertise; inexperienced buyers often underestimate operational challenges. Due diligence in distressed situations is complicated by poor records and time pressure but remains essential.

**Related terms:** [IBC](#what-is-ibc-insolvency-and-bankruptcy-code), [Liquidation Value](#what-is-liquidation-value), [Turnaround](#what-is-a-turnaround)

## What is Gratuity?

Gratuity is a statutory retirement benefit payable to employees in India who complete five or more years of continuous service. Under the Payment of Gratuity Act 1972, employers must pay departing employees 15 days wages for each completed year of service, capped at ₹20 lakh (subject to periodic increases). Gratuity liability is a significant employee-related obligation in acquisitions.

Gratuity due diligence examines: whether the company is covered under the Act (10+ employees triggers coverage), historical gratuity payments, current gratuity liability valuation (actuarial calculations for ongoing businesses), funding status (gratuity trust or insurance), and compliance with accounting standards (AS 15/Ind AS 19 requires recognition as liability). Unfunded gratuity represents a hidden liability that buyers assume in share purchases. Common issues include: underprovisioned gratuity, lack of actuarial valuations, and non-funded obligations. SPAs should include gratuity representations and ensure adequate provisioning. Buyers often require sellers to fully fund gratuity liabilities or adjust purchase price for underfunding before closing.

**Related terms:** [PF/ESI](#what-is-pfesi-compliance), [Employee Liabilities](#what-are-employee-liabilities), [Due Diligence](#what-is-due-diligence)

## What is Labor Due Diligence?

Labor due diligence examines all employee-related aspects of a target business including: workforce composition, compensation structures, statutory compliance (PF, ESI, gratuity, minimum wages), pending labor disputes, union relationships, and contingent worker arrangements. Labor issues can create significant post-acquisition liabilities and operational challenges—thorough labor diligence is essential for manufacturing and service businesses with large workforces.

Key labor due diligence areas for Indian acquisitions: employee registers and service records, salary structures and pending arrears, provident fund and ESI compliance (verify through portal), gratuity provisioning and funding, labor law registrations (Shops Act, Factories Act), standing orders compliance, pending labor court cases, union agreements and history, contract labor compliance, and sexual harassment committee requirements. Common issues include: misclassification of employees as contractors, PF/ESI coverage gaps, outdated standing orders, and pending industrial disputes. Labor liabilities can be substantial—one company discovered ₹2 crore in underpaid PF during diligence. Specific indemnification for labor matters and employee-related compliance is standard.

**Related terms:** [PF/ESI](#what-is-pfesi-compliance), [Gratuity](#what-is-gratuity), [Due Diligence](#what-is-due-diligence)

## What is IP Due Diligence?

Intellectual Property (IP) due diligence examines the target company's intellectual property assets, including trademarks, patents, copyrights, trade secrets, and domain names. IP diligence verifies ownership, validity, protection scope, infringement risks, and licensing arrangements. For technology, brand-driven, and innovation-based businesses, IP often represents core value requiring careful assessment.

Key IP due diligence areas include: trademark registrations and pending applications with Controller General of Patents, patent portfolio and protection timelines, copyright registrations for software and creative works, domain name ownership verification, third-party license agreements and compliance, employee IP assignment agreements, and infringement history (both by and against the company). Common issues include: unregistered trademarks vulnerable to challenge, expired patent protections, software using unlicensed components, and employee IP ownership disputes. In India, IP transfers in share purchases occur automatically; asset purchases require specific IP assignment documentation. IP warranties and indemnification for infringement claims are standard SPA provisions. Material IP concerns warrant specialist IP attorney review.

**Related terms:** [Intangible Asset](#what-is-an-intangible-asset), [Due Diligence](#what-is-due-diligence), [Trademark](#what-is-a-trademark)

## What is WACC (Weighted Average Cost of Capital)?

Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC) represents the blended cost of all capital sources—debt and equity—weighted by their proportion in the company's capital structure. WACC serves as the discount rate in DCF valuations, reflecting the return required by all capital providers. For Indian private companies, WACC typically ranges from 14-25% depending on risk profile.

WACC formula: (Weight of Equity × Cost of Equity) + (Weight of Debt × After-tax Cost of Debt). Cost of equity is estimated using models like CAPM, adjusted for size premiums and country risk for Indian markets. Cost of debt is observable from existing borrowings or market rates. Small Indian businesses typically have higher WACC due to: elevated country risk premiums, size-related risk adjustments, limited diversification, and owner dependency. Higher WACC means future cash flows are discounted more heavily, reducing present value. WACC assumptions significantly impact DCF valuations—changing WACC from 18% to 20% can reduce valuation by 10-15%. Sensitivity analysis testing different WACC scenarios is standard practice.

**Related terms:** [DCF](#what-is-dcf-discounted-cash-flow), [Discount Rate](#what-is-a-discount-rate), [Terminal Value](#what-is-terminal-value)

## What are Transaction Costs?

Transaction costs are expenses incurred to complete an acquisition beyond the purchase price—including advisor fees, legal costs, due diligence expenses, stamp duty, regulatory filings, and financing charges. Transaction costs typically range from 3-8% of deal value for Indian SME acquisitions, representing significant additional investment that buyers should budget.

Common transaction cost components: legal fees (₹3-15 lakh for SME deals), accounting/due diligence fees (₹2-10 lakh), business broker commissions (8-12% of purchase price), valuation reports (₹1-5 lakh), stamp duty on share transfers (0.25-0.75%), ROC filing fees (nominal), bank financing fees (1-2% of loan amount), and insurance (if R&W insurance used). Larger transactions have higher absolute costs but lower percentages. Buyers should estimate transaction costs early and include them in financing plans. Sellers also incur costs (legal, tax advisory, broker fees) that affect their net proceeds. Negotiations sometimes allocate specific costs between parties. Transaction cost tax treatment varies—some are deductible, others capitalized.

**Related terms:** [Stamp Duty](#what-is-stamp-duty-on-share-transfer), [Closing](#what-is-closing-in-ma), [Acquisition Financing](#what-is-acquisition-financing)

## What is a Merger?

A merger is a legal combination of two or more companies into a single entity—either one company absorbing another (absorption merger) or forming a new company altogether (consolidation merger). Unlike acquisitions where a buyer purchases a seller, mergers involve negotiated combinations where shareholders of both entities receive shares in the combined company. In India, mergers are governed by Companies Act 2013 and require NCLT approval.

Merger process involves: board approvals from both companies, shareholder and creditor meetings (75% shareholder approval required), NCLT petition and hearing, regulatory clearances (RBI, SEBI, CCI as applicable), and final NCLT order. The NCLT process typically takes 6-12 months and involves legal costs of ₹10-25 lakh. Mergers are common for: combining group companies for efficiency, reorganizing corporate structures, and true combinations of equals. Tax implications include potential capital gains for shareholders and business restructuring considerations. Section 233 provides faster merger routes for small companies. Post-merger integration determines ultimate success—combining cultures, systems, and operations requires careful planning.

**Related terms:** [NCLT](#what-is-nclt-national-company-law-tribunal), [Demerger](#what-is-a-demerger), [Amalgamation](#what-is-amalgamation)

## What is a Demerger?

A demerger (also called spin-off) separates one or more business divisions from a company into separate independent entities. Shareholders of the original company typically receive proportionate shares in the new entities. Demergers help focus operations, unlock value from underperforming divisions, or prepare specific businesses for sale. In India, demergers require NCLT approval under Companies Act 2013 and have specific tax implications under Income Tax Act.

Common demerger scenarios: separating real estate from operating businesses, spinning off digital ventures, isolating regulated businesses, and restructuring before IPOs or sales. Tax-neutral demergers require meeting specific conditions—undertaking transfer, business continuity, and shareholder consideration requirements under Section 2(19AA). Stamp duty on property transfers in demergers varies by state. SEBI has specific rules for listed company demergers. For acquisitions, demergers enable buyers to acquire only desired business portions without purchasing entire companies. Pre-sale demergers can streamline transactions and improve valuations by separating core businesses from non-core assets. Demerger planning requires coordinated legal, tax, and business strategy advice.

**Related terms:** [Merger](#what-is-a-merger), [NCLT](#what-is-nclt-national-company-law-tribunal), [Corporate Restructuring](#what-is-corporate-restructuring)
